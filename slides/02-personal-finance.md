# The Money Waterfall

## Where every dollar should go *before* you spend it

<!-- There's a correct order of operations for money, and almost nobody is taught it. This is the difference between freedom and a lifetime of "I can't afford to take that risk." I built this for US students, but I've included a full parallel track for India so it's equally actionable for everyone. -->

---

<!-- _class: statement -->

# Discipline doesn't make you boring. It buys you **options.**

<!-- This is the whole talk in one line. Money in the bank is permission to say no, and permission to bet on yourself. I want you to leave thinking of saving not as deprivation but as buying freedom. -->

---

## Why I can say this

- Invested aggressively on a **military salary** — not a glamorous one
- The result: a **~2-year runway**
- That runway lets me leave a stable career and take real risk

<!-- I invested hard on a modest Navy salary and built roughly a two-year runway. As I write this I have a 6-day-old daughter, and that runway is exactly what lets me leave a stable career and take entrepreneurial risk without endangering my family. None of this required a big income — it required order and consistency. -->

---

## The book under everything

> *The Compound Effect* — Darren Hardy

- Small, consistent actions, **ruthlessly repeated**
- Unstoppable over time

<!-- This whole talk is one application of The Compound Effect. Small consistent actions, repeated without drama, beat heroic one-time efforts every time. Keep that lens on as we go through the mechanics. -->

# Money is **permission.**

### Part 1
# The mindset before the mechanics

<!-- Before any account or instrument, four mindset shifts. Get these and the rest is just plumbing — the mechanics won't save you if the mindset is wrong. -->

---

## Mechanics fail without mindset

- The accounts are **easy**
- The **habits** are the hard part
- Mindset comes **first** for a reason

<!-- I'm front-loading mindset on purpose. The accounts and percentages are the easy part — anyone can open a Roth in ten minutes. What actually decides your outcome is whether you keep showing up. So we fix the operating system before we install the apps. -->

---

## Pay yourself first

- Invest **off the top**
- Spend what's left
- **Never** the reverse

<!-- Most people spend first and invest whatever survives — which is usually nothing. Flip it: the investment comes out the moment you get paid, and you live on the remainder. This one reversal is responsible for most of my results. -->

---

## Live below your means — on purpose

- The gap between **earn** and **spend**...
- ...*is* your freedom fund

<!-- Living below your means isn't punishment — the gap you create is literally the fuel for everything else. On purpose is the key phrase: it's a deliberate choice to keep that gap wide, not an accident of a frugal month. -->

---

## Time is the cheat code

- Money invested at **20 vs 30** isn't twice as much
- It's **many times** as much
- Compounding rewards the **early and patient**

<!-- The single biggest variable isn't how much you invest, it's how early. A dollar at 20 versus 30 isn't double — it's many times more because of extra compounding cycles. You are at the most valuable moment you will ever have for this; the cheat code expires a little every year. -->

---

## Assets vs liabilities

> *Rich Dad Poor Dad* — Kiyosaki

- **Assets** put money in your pocket
- **Liabilities** take it out
- Buy assets **first**

<!-- Kiyosaki's framing is simple and durable: assets feed you, liabilities drain you. Most people buy liabilities they think are assets — the financed car, the too-big house. Buy real assets first; the lifestyle comes later. -->

### Part 2
# The US order of operations

<!-- Now the waterfall itself. Fill each bucket before moving to the next one. I'll give you the why for each rung, because the why is what makes you stick to the order. -->

---

<!-- _class: statement -->

# Fill each bucket **before** the next one.

<!-- Picture a tiered fountain. Water fills the top bowl, then overflows to the next. Your money works the same way: don't dribble into bucket six while bucket two sits empty. -->

---

## The whole waterfall, top to bottom

1. High-interest **debt**
2. 401(k) **match**
3. **Roth** IRA
4. **HSA**
5. Max **Traditional**
6. **Taxable** brokerage

<!-- Here's the full map before we zoom into each rung. Six buckets, in order, top to bottom. Don't memorize the accounts — memorize the logic, which I'll repeat at the bottom. Let's take them one at a time. -->

---

## Rung 1 — High-interest debt

- Credit cards at **20–25%**
- A **guaranteed, tax-free** negative return
- No investment reliably beats that

<!-- Paying off a 22% card is a guaranteed, tax-free 22% return — better than almost any investment on earth, with zero risk. This is rung one for everyone, in every country. Until this is gone, investing elsewhere is just borrowing at 22% to earn maybe 8%. -->

---

## Rung 1 — Avalanche vs snowball

- **Avalanche:** highest rate first *(optimal math)*
- **Snowball:** smallest balance first *(fast win)*
- The best method is **the one you'll finish**

<!-- Two methods. The avalanche — highest interest rate first — is mathematically optimal. The snowball — smallest balance first — gives you a quick psychological win and momentum. I won't tell you the math is wrong, but the best plan is the one you actually complete. Pick for your personality. -->

---

## Rung 2 — 401(k) to the match

- A **50–100% instant, guaranteed** return
- **Free money**
- Nothing else competes with a match

<!-- If your employer matches, that's an instant 50 to 100% return before the market does anything. It is the single best deal on this list. Never, ever leave the match on the table — it's a raise you're choosing to decline. -->

---

## Rung 3 — Max a Roth IRA

- Pay tax **now**
- Grows and comes out **tax-free forever**
- Most powerful when **young and low-bracket**

<!-- With a Roth you pay tax today and never again — decades of growth come out completely tax-free. It's most powerful exactly when you're young and in a low bracket, because you're locking in today's low rate. This is one of the best gifts the US tax code gives young people. -->

---

## Rung 4 — Max an HSA

- The only **triple-tax-advantaged** account in America
- Deductible in · grows tax-free · out tax-free for medical
- After 59½ it acts like an **IRA**

<!-- If you have a high-deductible plan, the HSA is the only triple-tax-advantaged account in America: deductible going in, tax-free growth, tax-free out for medical. After 59 and a half it behaves like an IRA for anything. It's a stealth retirement account hiding inside your health plan. -->

---

## Rung 5 — Max Traditional

- Tax-**deferred** now lowers this year's taxable income
- Pay tax **later** in retirement
- Ideally at a **lower rate**

<!-- Now fill the rest of your Traditional 401(k) or IRA. This lowers your taxable income today and you pay tax later, ideally in a lower bracket in retirement. It comes after Roth and HSA because deferred beats nothing, but tax-free beats deferred. -->

---

## Rung 6 — Taxable brokerage

- No special tax shelter
- But **total flexibility**
- No penalties · invest in anything

<!-- Last comes the ordinary brokerage account. No tax shelter, but total flexibility — no withdrawal penalties, invest in anything, any time. This is where money goes once every advantaged bucket above is full. -->

---

<!-- _class: statement -->

# **Guaranteed** > **tax-free** > **tax-deferred** > **taxable**

<!-- This is the throughline — memorize it. Chase guaranteed returns first (debt payoff, the match), then tax-free growth (Roth, HSA), then tax-deferred (Traditional), then plain taxable. You're optimizing for certainty first, then tax efficiency. -->

---

### Part 3
# The India parallel

<!-- Same logic, different instruments. For students in India the waterfall maps cleanly — one important caveat up front, then rung by rung. -->

---

## The same six rungs — in India

1. High-interest **debt**
2. **EPF** match
3. **PPF / ELSS**
4. **80D** health insurance
5. **NPS**
6. Taxable **equity / funds**

<!-- Here's the Indian map alongside the US one. Notice it's the same six rungs in the same order — only the instruments change. Debt, the match, the Roth-equivalent, the health break, the deferred account, then taxable. -->

---

## One caveat first

- The **new tax regime** removes most of these deductions
- These shine under the **old regime**
- Choose **deliberately**

<!-- Crucial caveat before we start: India's new tax regime strips out most of the deductions I'm about to describe. These instruments shine under the old regime. So the meta-move is to choose your regime deliberately based on whether you'll actually use these deductions. -->

---

## India Rung 1 — High-interest debt

- Credit cards at **36–42% APR**
- Personal loans
- Universal · **identical** reasoning

<!-- Same rung one, even uglier numbers — Indian credit cards run 36 to 42% APR. Kill these and personal loans before anything else. The reasoning is identical: a guaranteed, tax-free return no investment can match. -->

---

## India Rung 2 — EPF to the match

- The **"free match"** rung
- Employer matches your **Provident Fund** contribution
- Capture it **fully**

<!-- This is India's version of the 401(k) match. Your employer matches your EPF — Provident Fund — contribution. Capture every rupee of it; it's the same free-money logic as rung two in the US. -->

---

## India Rung 3 — PPF / ELSS

- **PPF:** *EEE* — tax-free in, growth, and out *(15-yr)*
- **ELSS:** equity fund, shortest 80C lock-in *(3-yr)* + upside
- Share the **₹1.5 lakh** 80C cap

<!-- This is the Roth-style rung. PPF is EEE — exempt, exempt, exempt — contributions, growth and withdrawals all tax-free, the closest thing India has to a Roth, over a 15-year horizon. ELSS is an equity mutual fund with the shortest 80C lock-in, just 3 years, plus market upside. Note: EPF, PPF and ELSS all share the same ₹1.5 lakh Section 80C cap. -->

---

## India Rung 4 — Health insurance (80D)

- No exact HSA in India
- **Section 80D** deducts health premiums
- Get covered **young and cheap**

<!-- India has no exact HSA equivalent, but Section 80D lets you deduct health-insurance premiums — the nearest tax break tied to healthcare. Get covered while you're young and premiums are cheap. It maps to the HSA rung in spirit. -->

---

## India Rung 5 — NPS

- An **extra ₹50,000** deduction
- Under Section **80CCD(1B)**
- *On top of* the ₹1.5L 80C limit

<!-- NPS gives you an additional ₹50,000 deduction under Section 80CCD(1B), and crucially it's on top of the ₹1.5 lakh 80C limit — not inside it. This is the tax-deferred retirement rung, mirroring the Traditional 401(k). -->

---

## India Rung 6 — Direct equity / funds

- Full **flexibility** once advantaged buckets are full
- Direct equity or mutual funds
- Mind the **LTCG** rules

<!-- Finally, taxable: direct equity and mutual funds, for full flexibility once the advantaged buckets are full. Just mind the long-term capital gains rules. Same position as the US brokerage — last, not first. -->

---

<!-- _class: statement -->

# Same principle everywhere — and **always** capture the match first.

<!-- Whichever country you're in: guaranteed beats tax-free beats tax-deferred beats taxable, and you always capture the employer match first. The instruments change; the order never does. -->

<!-- A few hard-won shortcuts I wish someone had handed me at twenty. -->

## Automate everything

- Invest on **payday**
- *Before* you can spend it

<!-- Willpower is unreliable; automation isn't. Set the transfer to fire on payday before the money ever feels like yours. This is pay-yourself-first turned into a system you can't forget. -->

## Boring index funds win

- You are **not** out-trading the market

## Don't skip rungs

- My **two rentals** (VA & SC) came *after* the basics
- Not **instead** of them

<!-- I own two rental properties, in Virginia and South Carolina. They came after the foundation was built, not in place of it. Don't chase the exciting thing while skipping the unglamorous rungs underneath it — the exciting stuff only works because the base is there. -->
