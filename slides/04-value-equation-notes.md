# The Value Equation — Talking Notes

> One section per slide. Bullets to discuss — talk to the room, don't read them. Slide numbers match the deck.

## Slide 1 — The Only Equation in Business That Matters (title)
- Open warm: "Value isn't a vibe. It's a fraction. Once you can see the fraction, you can engineer demand for almost anything."
- Set the shape of the session: ~45 min of talk, then ~15 min of live product teardowns done together with the room.
- Promise: by the end, everyone leaves with a single lens they can point at any product — their own idea or a company they're critiquing.
- Tone-setter: this is the one framework I personally hold up as a guiding light when deciding what to build.

## Slide 2 — Value isn't a vibe. It's a fraction.
- The whole thesis compressed. Most people treat "value" as mystical or subjective; it's not.
- It's a ratio with four inputs, and you can move every single one of them on purpose.
- The shift I want to create today: stop guessing why something sells and start engineering it.
- Transition: "So who taught me to see it this way?"

## Slide 3 — I've watched hundreds of hours of Hormozi.
- Honest credibility: I've watched hundreds of hours of Alex Hormozi tearing down real businesses.
- I can't do it as well as he can — but I can get maybe 80% of the way there.
- And 80% is more than enough to evaluate your own idea or critique a company's marketing.
- The promise of today is to hand you that same 80% lens — it's learnable in an hour.

## Slide 4 — My one filter for what to build.
- This equation is my build/don't-build filter. Before code, before a single cold call, I run the idea through the fraction.
- "If a product idea doesn't survive this equation, I don't build it."
- It's saved me from a lot of bad ideas — the kind that feel exciting but score terribly on the levers.
- Frame it as discipline: a great-feeling idea that's weak on the fraction is a trap.

## Slide 5 — Part 1: The equation (divider)
- We're going to build the fraction piece by piece — don't write it down yet, just watch it assemble.
- Source it up front: this is from Hormozi's *$100M Offers*.
- Tease: by the end of this part you'll never un-see it in the wild.

## Slide 6 — Straight from the source.
- Full credit: the equation is Alex Hormozi's, from *$100M Offers*. I didn't invent it; I live by it.
- If you take one book away from today, make it this one — short, blunt, changed how I see every business.
- *$100M Leads* is its companion (traffic side); we'll touch it later. Today is the offer side.
- Transition: "Here's the whole thing, then we take it apart."

## Slide 7 — Value = (Dream × Likelihood) ÷ (Time Delay × Effort)
- Read it out loud, slowly. Two things on top you want BIG; two things on the bottom you want SMALL.
- To create value: push the top up, push the bottom down. That's the entire game.
- Numerator is desire and belief; denominator is friction. Value explodes when desire/belief are high and friction is near zero.
- Everything else in the session is just detail on these four terms.

## Slide 8 — Four levers. That's it.
- Name them: Dream Outcome (↑), Perceived Likelihood (↑), Time Delay (↓), Effort & Sacrifice (↓).
- Two you maximize, two you minimize. Memorize these four words — every offer gets scored on exactly these.
- Preview the punchline: businesses obsess over one of these and ignore the other three. Which one? Hold on.
- We'll take each lever in turn now.

## Slide 9 — Lever 1: Dream Outcome ↑ (divider)
- This is the promise — the destination the customer is buying their way toward.
- Core question: how badly does the customer want the result?
- The bigger and more vivid the dream, the more value — but watch where I'm going with this.

## Slide 10 — Dream Outcome ↑
- Sell the destination, not the vehicle. Nobody wants a drill; they want the hole — really, the finished shelf and the tidy home.
- This is the lever everyone instinctively reaches for: just make a bigger, bolder promise.
- Examples: "get fit" vs "look incredible at your wedding"; "save money" vs "retire five years early."
- Plant the flag: this is the WRONG place to compete, and I'll show you why in a couple of minutes.

## Slide 11 — Lever 2: Perceived Likelihood ↑ (divider)
- The question shifts from "do they want it?" to "do they believe it'll work FOR THEM?"
- This is the lever almost nobody pulls — and it's the cheapest one to move.
- Set it up as the secret weapon of the whole talk.

## Slide 12 — Perceived Likelihood ↑
- The dream is great, but do they believe THEY specifically will get it? That's perceived likelihood of achievement.
- You move it with guarantees, proof, testimonials, case studies, track record, live demonstrations.
- The key line: this is usually the CHEAPEST lever to move and the MOST neglected.
- A money-back guarantee can cost almost nothing yet double conversion. We come back to this constantly in the teardowns.

## Slide 13 — The cheapest lever to move. (statement)
- Its own moment because it's the single most actionable idea in the session.
- Perceived likelihood: cheapest to move, most neglected. A guarantee, a testimonial, a demo cost almost nothing.
- Yet most businesses pour everything into a bigger promise and forget to make anyone BELIEVE it.
- If you remember one lever from today, make it this one. Skeptical markets are won here.

## Slide 14 — Lever 3: Time Delay ↓ (divider)
- Time delay = the gap between buying and getting the result.
- Core rule: faster is always more valuable. Speed is a feature, not a nicety.

## Slide 15 — Time Delay ↓
- "Lose weight in 90 days" beats "in 2 years," even if the end state is identical — we discount the future heavily.
- This is why "instant," "today," "in 5 minutes," "overnight" are some of the most powerful words in marketing.
- Tie to behavior: people will pay a real premium to shorten the wait, even for the same outcome.
- Quick ask the room: name a product you bought mostly because it was FAST.

## Slide 16 — Lever 4: Effort & Sacrifice ↓ (divider)
- Effort & sacrifice = everything the customer has to give up: work, time, willpower, risk, money on the side.
- Less effort, less sacrifice → more value. Done-for-you beats do-it-yourself.

## Slide 17 — Effort & Sacrifice ↓
- The ladder: done-for-you > done-with-you > do-it-yourself. Every step you remove from the customer's plate raises value.
- This is the entire premium-pricing logic of services — people pay enormous amounts to NOT do the work themselves.
- Risk is a sacrifice too; a guarantee removes the "what if it fails" cost (note the overlap with likelihood).
- Transition to recap: "We've built the fraction. Let's zoom back out."

## Slide 18 — Top up. Bottom down. (statement)
- Quick recap before the big insight: Dream and Likelihood up; Time and Effort down.
- Have them say it back: "top up, bottom down." That's the mechanical instruction inside the fraction.
- Now pivot hard into where most businesses get it wrong.

## Slide 19 — The mistake almost everyone makes.
- Most important insight in the talk: most businesses compete on ONE lever — Dream Outcome.
- They just keep making a bigger, bolder promise. "Even better results!" It's an arms race everyone's already in.
- The problem: when everyone shouts the same dream, the dream stops differentiating you. You become indistinguishable.
- Set up the fix: the edge is elsewhere.

## Slide 20 — The leverage is in the other three. (statement)
- The real edge lives in the three levers nobody else is touching: believability, speed, ease.
- That's Perceived Likelihood, Time Delay, and Effort & Sacrifice.
- When a competitor sells a slightly bigger dream and you sell the SAME dream but more believable, faster, and easier — you win. Every time.
- This is the strategic heart of the session.

## Slide 21 — Believability · Speed · Ease.
- Three words to carry out of the room: believability, speed, ease.
- Mapping: believability = likelihood; speed = killing time delay; ease = killing effort & sacrifice.
- Practical habit: when you catch yourself reaching only for a bigger promise, stop — can I make it more believable, faster, or easier instead?
- Usually that's the cheaper and bigger win. Now let's prove it on real products.

## Slide 22 — Part 2: Live teardowns (divider)
- Now we make it real. I'll run four very different products through the equation out loud, with you.
- For each: rate the four levers, find the weak one, name the fix, identify the marketing pathway.
- The recurring question I'll keep asking: which lever is this company ACTUALLY selling? Often it's the wrong one.
- Invite participation — this should be a conversation, not a lecture.

## Slide 23 — Teardown 1: SaaS
- A scheduling/booking tool. Dream Outcome: "you'll never lose a booking again."
- Likelihood lever: onboarding quality, integrations that prove it fits their stack, social proof from similar businesses.
- Time delay: setup friction is the enemy — kill it. Effort: automation — the tool does the work for them.
- Marketing pathway: free trial, ready-made templates, "set up in 5 minutes." Notice that pathway sells SPEED and EASE, not a bigger dream.

## Slide 24 — Teardown 2: AI / automation (my lane)
- An automation or AI service — my lane. Dream: "your tedious, repetitive work just disappears."
- The entire pitch is collapsing Time Delay and Effort & Sacrifice to near zero. That's WHY automation sells so well.
- It attacks the two bottom levers directly and hard — the value of the fraction shoots up.
- Marketing pathway: before/after demos, hours-saved numbers, an ROI calculator. You literally quantify the time and effort you removed.

## Slide 25 — Teardown 3: Physical good
- A mattress or a supplement. Dream: better sleep, better health.
- Big lever here is Perceived Likelihood — moved by reviews, a trial period, and a money-back guarantee that directly attacks perceived RISK.
- The buyer's real question is "will this work for ME?" The guarantee answers it.
- Marketing pathway: the 100-night trial. Not a gimmick — it's removing effort and risk from the buyer while cranking believability. Cheapest lever, biggest move.

## Slide 26 — Teardown 4: High-ticket service
- Consulting or coaching. Dream: a genuine transformation.
- Done-for-you crushes Effort & Sacrifice — the client doesn't have to figure it out, do the reps, or carry the risk alone.
- That's precisely why these services command premium pricing: they obliterate the bottom-right lever.
- The line: people pay the most to do the least.

## Slide 27 — "Which lever is this company actually selling?" (statement)
- The question I want them firing at every ad for the rest of their lives.
- Pick any company — look at the homepage and ads. Which of the four levers are they leading with?
- Very often the marketing emphasizes the WRONG lever: shouting a bigger dream at a skeptical market that actually needs proof, or selling speed to a market anxious about risk.
- This diagnostic is most of what a "teardown" actually is.

## Slide 28 — Part 3: What actually sells (divider)
- The offer is only half the battle. Now: pathways, and which lever to lead with.
- A great offer with no traffic fails; great traffic with a weak offer fails too.
- We'll connect today's offer work to the traffic lanes from the sales session.

## Slide 29 — Offer ≠ traffic.
- The equation we built today is the OFFER engine.
- The four lead lanes from the sales session — warm/cold × one-to-one/one-to-many — are the TRAFFIC engine.
- A great offer fails with no traffic; great traffic fails with a weak offer. The two halves must meet.
- Today is offer day — just know the second half exists and where it plugs in.

## Slide 30 — Sell the lever they're most anxious about.
- The rule for WHAT actually sells: lead with the lever the customer is most anxious about.
- Skeptical market (don't believe it works) → sell likelihood: proof, guarantees. Impatient market → sell speed.
- Busy/overwhelmed market → sell ease, done-for-you. Aspirational market that already believes → then, and only then, sell the dream.
- Diagnose the anxiety first. The right lever is the one that calms the specific fear in front of you.

## Slide 31 — Find your one growth lever.
- Traffic side: *$100M Leads* (Hormozi) plus Sean Ellis's *Hacking Growth*.
- Find your ONE growth lever — the single channel or loop that's working — instrument it with data, double down.
- Don't diversify channels until that one is genuinely saturated.
- Beginner death: spreading across five channels and doing all five badly. Focus beats diversification when you're small.

## Slide 32 — Pigheaded discipline.
- Chet Holmes, *The Ultimate Sales Machine*. His phrase: "pigheaded discipline."
- Pigheaded discipline on a few fundamentals beats chasing every shiny new tactic.
- Pairs with the equation: the four levers ARE the fundamentals. Master moving them; ignore the noise.
- The boring basics, done relentlessly, win.

## Slide 33 — Part 4: How I run my own startup through it (divider)
- Make it concrete and personal: my AI receptionist business, scored live on the four levers.
- Including — honestly — where it's weak right now.
- I'd rather show the in-progress version than a polished case study; it's more useful and more believable.

## Slide 34 — My AI receptionist business.
- Dream: "you'll never miss a customer call again" — for an SMB, every missed call is lost revenue.
- Likelihood: I move it with real demos they can hear, plus a trial. Time delay: we can be live in a day.
- Effort: done-for-you — we set the whole thing up for them.
- On paper it scores well on speed and effort, so those are the levers I lead with on cold calls.

## Slide 35 — Where it's weak — and what I'm fixing.
- Honest part: my weakest lever right now is Perceived Likelihood.
- Cold SMB owners don't yet believe an AI will handle THEIR specific calls well.
- What I'm fixing: more proof — recorded demos with real businesses, a stronger guarantee, named case studies.
- The irony: it's the cheapest, most-neglected lever — and I was neglecting it too. The framework isn't theory to me; I'm using it this week.

## Slide 36 — Your turn: Score your idea (divider)
- Transition into the exercise — energy up.
- Everyone writes first, then I score several out loud, rapid-fire.

## Slide 37 — Write your idea as the fraction.
- The exercise: take your own idea — or a product you love — and write it as the four-lever fraction.
- Score each lever, circle the single weakest one, propose ONE concrete fix to move it. Just one.
- Give them a few quiet minutes to actually write.
- Remind them: name the marketing pathway the fix implies, not just the lever.

## Slide 38 — Rapid-fire.
- Hands up — call out your product and your weakest lever; I'll teardown a handful live, on the spot.
- This is where the 80% lens gets contagious — they watch how fast diagnosis happens once the fraction is in your head.
- Push each one to also name the marketing pathway, not just the lever.
- Keep it fast and fun; momentum matters more than perfection.
