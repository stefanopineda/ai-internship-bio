# You Don't Rise to the Occasion

## Leadership under pressure, from a submarine

<!--
TALK TRACK — open here.
Welcome them. I spent years on a submarine, where "freezing up" isn't embarrassing — it can get everyone killed. That stakes-level taught me what leadership actually is.
The myth says heroes summon something extra in a crisis. I'm here to break that myth.
Frame: ~45 min of talk, then 15 of Q&A. Keep all sub content at the unclassified engineering level — buoyancy, ship systems, human performance.
-->

---

<!-- _class: statement -->

# Freezing isn't embarrassing here. It's fatal.

<!--
On a submarine, "not knowing the procedure" or freezing up isn't an awkward moment. It can get everyone aboard killed.
That stakes-level strips away the fluff. It forces a brutally honest answer to "what is leadership actually for?"
Everything I'm about to say was forged at that stakes-level — then I'll bring it back down to your interview, your pitch, your startup.
-->

---

<!-- _class: statement -->

# The myth: in a crisis, heroes rise.

<!--
We're sold a story: when the alarm goes off, the hero finds another gear and rises to the moment.
It's a great movie. It's a terrible operating model.
I've lived real crises, and that is not what happens.
-->

---

<!-- _class: statement -->

> You don't rise to the occasion. You fall to your level of preparation.

<!--
This is the whole talk in one line. The military version: "you do not rise to the occasion; you fall to your level of training."
When the alarm sounds you do not get smarter. You execute whatever you already burned into muscle memory.
So the entire job of a leader is to raise that level — in advance, in the calm.
-->

---

## Calm is not a personality trait.

**Calm is a product of preparation.**

<!--
People watch a calm operator and assume it's temperament — "they're just wired that way."
No. The calm you see under pressure was manufactured beforehand, through reps.
You don't get to be calm in the moment. You get to be as calm as your preparation already made you.
-->

---

<!-- _class: divider -->

### Part 1
# Preparation is the performance

<!--
Now let's make this concrete. Where does that high floor actually come from?
The answer is boring, repeatable, and the opposite of heroic.
-->

---

## Countless drills. On purpose boring.

Simulators. Casualties of every kind. Over and over.

<!--
I ran countless drills in simulators — steam-line ruptures, casualties of every kind — repeated until the response was automatic.
The drills are boring on purpose. Boring is the point. Boring means it's becoming reflex instead of a decision.
The drama is meant to be absorbed in the simulator so there's none left for the real thing.
-->

---

<!-- _class: statement -->

# Performance =
# (Preparation × Knowledge), executed calmly.

<!--
Here's my equation for clutch performance: Performance under pressure = (Preparation × Knowledge) executed calmly.
It's a product, not a sum — if either factor is near zero, the whole thing collapses, no matter how brave you feel.
Raise either factor in the calm, and your floor rises with it.
-->

---

## "Coming in clutch" is just a high floor.

*You prepared so well that your worst, most-stressed self is still good enough.*

<!--
From the outside, clutch looks like talent or courage. From the inside it's unglamorous: a high floor.
You prepared so thoroughly that even your worst, most-stressed self clears the bar.
You're not betting on your best day showing up. You're guaranteeing your bad day is good enough.
-->

---

<!-- _class: statement -->

# When did you crack? When did you nail it?

<!--
Question for the room: think of one time you cracked under pressure, and one time you absolutely nailed it.
Now ask: what was actually different? Be honest.
Almost every time, the answer isn't courage or mood — it's preparation. You'd done the reps, or you hadn't.
-->

### Part 2
# Sea stories: performing under pressure

<!--
Four real engineering casualties from the boat. Each one decided before the alarm, by training.

---

## Every outcome was decided *before* the alarm.

Buoyancy. Fluid systems. Valve failures. Human response.

<!--
Frame for all four stories: each is a real casualty told at the high engineering level — buoyancy, fluid systems, valve failures, human response.
The point that repeats every single time: the outcome was decided before the alarm ever sounded, by training.
Watch for it in each one — the moment of "success" is just preparation cashing out.
Reminder: keep all of this unclassified — engineering and human performance only.

<!-- _class: divider -->

### Sea story 1
# Hydraulic rupture

<!--
A high-pressure hydraulic system lets go. Sudden, loud, high-energy.
Reminder: unclassified engineering level only — fluid systems and human response, nothing operational.
-->

---

## Isolate. Control. Communicate.

*The trained response beats improvisation every time.*

<!--
When a high-pressure system ruptures, the trained sequence is isolate the source, control the affected system, communicate clearly across the team.
Improvising in that moment is how you make it worse. The procedure exists because someone already thought it through when nobody was bleeding adrenaline.
The win wasn't a hero. It was people doing the boring thing fast.
Reminder: keep this unclassified — fluid systems and human performance only.
-->

---

<!-- _class: divider -->

### Sea story 2
# Refrigerant leak

<!--
A refrigerant leak in a confined space — a genuine hazard to the people in the compartment.
Reminder: unclassified — confined-space hazard and human response, nothing more.
-->

---

## Confined space. Calm procedure wins.

*Panic is the actual hazard.*

<!--
Refrigerant in a confined space is a real human hazard. The leak is dangerous; panic is more dangerous.
Trained procedure — protect your people, ventilate, follow the steps — is what turns a scary event into a managed one.
The lesson repeats: calm procedure beats fast panic, and calm came from drilling it cold.
Reminder: keep this at the confined-space-hazard and human-performance level only.
-->

---

<!-- _class: divider -->

### Sea story 3
# Reverse-osmosis repairs

<!--
The water plant — reverse osmosis, the system that makes fresh water. Unglamorous, critical, always breaking.
Reminder: unclassified — this is just reliability and maintenance of a critical system.
-->

---

## Reliability is a leadership outcome.

The unglamorous grind of keeping critical gear alive.

<!--
The reverse-osmosis plant is the opposite of a dramatic casualty — it's the daily grind of keeping critical equipment alive.
There's no alarm, no movie moment, just relentless maintenance. But reliability is a leadership outcome you build with that grind.
Most of leadership isn't the crisis. It's the boring upkeep that prevents the crisis.
Reminder: keep this at the maintenance/reliability level only.
-->

### Sea story 4
# Loss of depth control

<!--
This is the one REAL casualty I personally had to manage — not a drill.

---

## Buoyancy. A valve that should hold — didn't.

*The one real casualty I managed. Not a drill.*

<!--
At the unclassified level: this is fundamentally about buoyancy and the failure behavior of remotely-operated valves.
When a system that should hold doesn't, the boat's control of its own depth is at stake. That's the physics — nothing more, and I'll keep it there.
This was the one real casualty I had to manage, not a simulation.
Reminder: physics and seamanship only — buoyancy and ROV failure modes. No tactics.
-->

---

## What made it go right was not heroics.

People falling back on trained procedure. Clear communication.

<!--
There was no hero moment. What made it go right was people falling back on trained procedure and communicating clearly.
The training absorbed the drama. We executed what we'd already burned in.
That's the thesis proven in the highest-stakes case I personally faced: we didn't rise — we fell to our level of preparation, and the floor was high enough.
Reminder: keep it at buoyancy / ROV-failure / human-performance level.
-->

---

<!-- _class: statement -->

# The drama is absorbed by the training.

<!--
The common thread across all four stories: every time it went right, it was because people performed the preparation.
The training soaked up the drama so the humans didn't have to manufacture courage on the spot.
That's the entire case for drilling the boring stuff.
-->

### Part 3
# Leading vs. managing

<!--
Now a distinction these students need named, because most people blur them and do neither well.
Both matter. They are not the same job.
-->

---

## Managing = systems, procedures, the known.

Checklist complete. Standard enforced. Trains running.

<!--
Management is about systems, procedures, and the known — keeping the trains running, the checklist complete, the standard enforced.
This is essential. The submarine runs on superb management; without it the boat doesn't float.
Do not let anyone tell you management is the lesser skill. It's just not the same skill as leadership.
-->

---

## Leading = people, the unknown.

Direction. Absorbing fear. Owning it when it breaks.

<!--
Leadership is about people and the unknown — setting direction, absorbing fear so your team can function, making the call with incomplete information, owning it when it goes wrong.
Notice "absorbing fear." In a casualty, the leader's job is to keep the humans from freezing while management keeps the procedure exact.
You're not managing the valve. You're leading the person turning it.
-->

---

## In a casualty, you need both at once.

*Management keeps the procedure exact. Leadership keeps the humans executing it.*

<!--
You don't pick one. You switch between them constantly, sometimes second to second.
In a casualty: management keeps the procedure exact; leadership keeps the humans executing it from freezing.
Great operators toggle so fast it looks like one thing. It's two.
-->

---

## Covey: right things vs. things right.

> Management is doing things right. Leadership is doing the right things.

<span class="attrib">— 7 Habits (Covey)</span>

<!--
Covey nails it: management is doing things right; leadership is doing the right things.
The classic image: management is the speed and form of climbing the ladder. Leadership is making sure the ladder is leaning against the right wall.
You can be flawlessly efficient at climbing the wrong wall. That's why you need both.
-->

### Part 4
# Delegation vs. abdication

<!--
Now I transplant the exact same lesson from the boat to a startup.
Via The E-Myth Revisited by Michael E. Gerber.
-->

---

## E-Myth: work ON it, not IN it.

> Most small businesses fail because the founder works *in* the business instead of *on* it.

<span class="attrib">— The E-Myth Revisited (Michael E. Gerber)</span>

<!--
Gerber's core insight: most small businesses fail because the founder works in the business — the technician doing the task — instead of on it, building the system that does the task.
On a submarine you cannot personally do every job; you build the system that does them. Same with a company.
The technician trap feels productive and quietly kills the business.
-->

---

## Delegation = system + standard + accountability.

**You hand off the task. You still own the outcome.**

<!--
Delegation is handing off a task with a clear system, a clear standard, and accountability — then verifying.
The leader still owns the outcome. Always. You delegate the task; you never delegate the ownership.
That verification step is the difference between trusting blindly and trusting safely.
-->

---

## Abdication = throw it over the wall and hope.

*No system. No standard. No follow-up.*

<!--
Abdication is throwing a task over the wall and hoping. No system, no standard, no follow-up.
This is how new founders get burned, then wrongly conclude "I have to do everything myself."
They didn't delegate and fail. They abdicated. Different diagnosis, different cure.
-->

---

<!-- _class: statement -->

# "I have to do everything myself" is a symptom of abdication.

<!--
When a founder tells me "I just have to do it all myself," I don't hear a personality. I hear a process failure.
They handed off tasks with no system, no standard, no follow-up — and got predictably burned.
The fix isn't to take it all back. It's to build the system that makes handing off safe — exactly what the boat forced me to do.
-->

---

## The boat taught me delegation at gunpoint.

*Trust trained watchstanders — but build and verify the system that makes the trust safe.*

<!--
The submarine taught me delegation at gunpoint. I had to trust trained watchstanders to execute under real risk.
But the trust wasn't faith — I built and verified the system that made it safe: the drills, the procedures, the qualifications.
You delegate the task; you never abdicate the ownership. That's exactly what a founder must learn.
-->

---

<!-- _class: statement -->

# Delegate the task. Never abdicate the ownership.

<!--
This is the line to remember from the whole business section.
Same idea on the boat and in the startup: hand off the doing, keep the owning.
If it breaks, it's yours. That's not a burden — it's the job.
-->

---

### Part 5
# Build your own high floor

<!--
So how do you, today, use any of this if you're never going near a submarine?
You build your own high floor. Same mechanism, lower stakes.
-->

---

## Pick the high-stakes moments. Drill them.

An interview. A pitch. A hard conversation.

<!--
Pick the situations you already know will be high-stakes — an interview, a pitch, a hard conversation with someone.
Then drill them before they happen. Rehearse out loud. Anticipate the casualty — the tough question, the objection, the moment it goes sideways.
You're running the simulator for your own life.
-->

---

## Raise the floor in the calm.

*So your stressed self inherits it.*

<!--
The whole move is timing: raise your knowledge and preparation in the calm times.
Then, when the pressure hits, your stressed self inherits a floor it didn't have to build under fire.
You will not be smarter in the moment. So get smarter before the moment. Every time.
-->

---

<!-- _class: statement -->

# Don't try to be a hero. Build a higher floor.

<!--
The reframe I want them to walk out with: stop waiting to rise, stop trying to be the hero.
Build the floor so high that even your worst day clears the bar.
That's not less ambitious than heroism. It's how heroism actually gets manufactured.
-->
