# Heads I Win, Tails I Don't Lose Much

## Building optionality *before* you need it

<!--
Open here. Welcome the room.
Today is about risk — but not the kind you're picturing. Not bravery, not gut, not "going big."
The real skill is structuring a bet so the downside is small and the upside is enormous, then playing it over and over.
Run: ~45 min of talking plus an exercise and open Q&A at the end.
-->

---

<!-- _class: statement -->

# In 16 months I leave a stable Navy career.

<!--
Here's my situation: I have a wife and a newborn, and in 16 months I'm walking away from a stable Navy paycheck to chase entrepreneurship.
Said out loud, that sounds reckless. Most people hear it and wince.
I want to convince you by the end that it's actually the *low-risk* move — because of work I did years ago.
-->

---

<!-- _class: statement -->

# It only sounds reckless.

<!--
The reason it isn't reckless is that I spent years quietly building the safety net that makes the bet small.
The leap looks dramatic from the outside. The preparation was invisible and boring.
That gap — between how a decision looks and how it's actually structured — is the whole talk.
-->

---

## The skill isn't taking risks.

> It's **structuring** them.

<!--
Let me name the misconception directly. People think entrepreneurs are risk-takers, adrenaline junkies.
The good ones are the opposite — they're obsessive about capping their downside.
Today I'll give you the framework I use, the investor who taught it to me, and how to apply it to the most valuable thing you own: your time.
-->

---

<!-- _class: divider -->

### Part 1
# The Dhandho Principle

<!--
This framework comes from a book called The Dhandho Investor by Mohnish Pabrai.
Dhandho is a Gujarati word — it means "endeavors that create wealth."
The community that coined it, the Patels, built fortunes on one simple, ruthless idea about risk.
-->

---

<!-- _class: statement -->

> **"Heads, I win; tails, I don't lose much."**

<!--
This is the whole philosophy in one sentence. Read it slowly.
If the coin comes up heads, I win big. If it comes up tails, I barely get hurt.
Notice it says nothing about being right more often. It's about making the payoffs lopsided. That's asymmetry.
-->

---

## The sweet spot most people run from.

**Low risk, high *uncertainty*.**

<!--
Here's the counterintuitive part. The best bets sit where risk is LOW but uncertainty is HIGH.
Most people flee uncertainty — a wide range of possible outcomes feels scary.
But that instinct is exactly why these bets are mispriced and available to you.
-->

---

## Risk ≠ uncertainty.

- **Risk** = permanent loss of capital
- **Uncertainty** = a wide range of outcomes

<!--
This distinction is the key that unlocks everything. They are not the same thing.
Risk is the chance you lose your shirt for good. Uncertainty is just not knowing which way things go.
When something is already priced for disaster, you can have enormous uncertainty with almost no real downside left to lose.
-->

---

## Pabrai's image: scrap value.

A business near its *scrap value* can't fall much further.

<!--
Pabrai pictures it like this. Imagine a business trading near what you'd get just selling off its parts — its scrap value.
The floor is already in. It can't fall much further, because there's almost nothing left to fall.
But if conditions improve even a little, the upside is multiples. Capped downside, open upside.
-->

---

<!-- _class: statement -->

# That's asymmetry.

<!--
This is the word I want you to leave with: asymmetry.
A bet where being wrong costs you a little and being right pays you a lot.
Hold that word — it comes back in every section of this talk.
-->

---

### Part 1, continued
# Pabrai lived it himself.

<!--
The reason I trust this framework is that the man who wrote it didn't just invest this way — he built his whole life this way.
Before he was a famous investor, he was a founder, just like you might be.
Let me walk you through his actual story, because it's a textbook calculated risk.
-->

---

## TransTech, 1991.

Founded his IT company with:
- ~**$30K** from his own 401(k)
- ~**$70K** of credit-card debt

<!--
In 1991 Pabrai started an IT services company called TransTech.
He funded it with about thirty thousand dollars from his own retirement account and around seventy thousand in credit-card debt.
On the surface that sounds insane — credit cards? But watch how he structured the downside.
-->

---

## The downside was *bounded*.

He could rebuild. The upside was open.

<!--
The money he risked was real, but it was finite and recoverable. He was young, skilled, and employable.
If it all went to zero, he could pay it down over time and start again. The loss was survivable, not permanent.
That's the difference between risk and ruin — he was nowhere near ruin.
-->

---

## And he had a floor.

> His old employer said they'd **take him back** if he failed.

<!--
Here's the detail I love. His former employer told him: if this doesn't work, you have a job here.
That's a literal floor under the downside. He could only ever land so low.
Once your downside is capped, all you have left to chase is upside. That is optionality.
-->

---

## Then he sold it.

- Bootstrapped to ~**$20M** in revenue
- Sold in **2000 for ~$20M**

<!--
He grew TransTech to around twenty million in revenue and sold it in the year 2000 for roughly twenty million dollars.
No outside venture money, no hype — bootstrapped.
That sale is what funded the next, even bigger chapter of his life.
-->

---

## Let's be honest about the number.

~$20M in 2000 ≈ ~**$37M** today.

<!--
I want to be precise and honest here, because you'll see inflated figures floating around online.
Twenty million dollars in 2000, adjusted for inflation, is roughly thirty-seven million in today's dollars — I'll show you the CPI math live.
It is NOT sixty million, which some retellings claim. Get your facts right; it makes the real story more credible, not less.
-->

---

## Then he ran the playbook in public.

Cloning Buffett — betting only where the **downside was already priced in**.

<!--
With that capital he started an investment fund and ran the exact same Dhandho logic in public markets.
He openly "cloned" Warren Buffett — copying great investors' best ideas rather than inventing his own.
And he only bet where the bad news was already baked into the price, so the floor was in. Same principle, bigger stage.
-->

### Part 2
# From money to time.

<!--
Now the move that makes this talk matter for everyone in this room, not just future investors.
The Dhandho lens isn't only about money.
Your time and your attention are capital too — and most people invest them terribly.
-->

---

<!-- _class: statement -->

# Your time is the asset you can't get back.

<!--
You can lose money and earn it back. You cannot earn back an hour.
So time is actually a more precious form of capital than money, and it deserves the same discipline.
Yet almost nobody applies any framework to how they spend it.
-->

---

## You are *always* building something.

Even when you don't mean to.

<!--
Every action you take is shaping your brain, your habits, your skill stack.
You are always under construction — the only question is whether you're building on purpose or by accident.
Two hours of doom-scrolling builds something too. Just not something you'd choose.
-->

---

## One question for any activity:

> "Is this building durable capacity, or just consuming hours?"

<!--
This is the filter I run things through. Is this growing a capability that compounds, or is it just burning time?
Not everything has to be productive — rest is real. But be honest about which bucket each thing is in.
Most regret comes from consuming hours while telling yourself you were building.
-->

---

## An example I live.

Spend the hour on a **one-off task**, or on an **automation / a YouTube video** that compounds?

<!--
Concrete case from my own week. I can knock out a one-off task in an hour, or I can spend that hour building an automation that saves me that hour forever — or a YouTube video that keeps working while I sleep.
The asset-building option is usually slower today and almost always wins over a year.
I deliberately pick the compounding option even when it's the harder choice right now.
-->

---

## Probabilistic thinking.

From Taleb's *Incerto* — *Antifragile · Fooled by Randomness · Skin in the Game*.

<!--
The mental toolkit for this comes from Nassim Taleb's Incerto series — Antifragile, Fooled by Randomness, and Skin in the Game.
The core habit: judge a decision by the full distribution of possible outcomes, not by the single one that happened to occur.
A good decision can have a bad outcome and still be a good decision. Don't confuse the two.
-->

---

## Seek asymmetry. Be antifragile.

- Bets where being **wrong costs little**, **right pays a lot**
- Structure life so **volatility helps** you

<!--
Two takeaways from Taleb. First, hunt for asymmetry — the same lopsided payoff Pabrai chases.
Second, be antifragile: arrange your life so that shocks and volatility actually make you stronger instead of wiping you out.
Fragile things break under stress. Antifragile things gain from it. Aim to be the latter.
-->

### Part 3
# The Casino Question

<!--
This next reframe is the single most useful question I know for deciding what to do with your time.
It comes from thinking about casinos.
Once it's in your head, you can't un-see it — you'll start sorting everything in your life through it.
-->

---

<!-- _class: statement -->

> Are you the **gambler**, or are you the **house**?

<!--
In every activity, skill, and venture — ask: am I the gambler, or am I the house?
The gambler plays games where the odds are structurally against them. Sometimes they win, but time grinds them down.
The house plays games where time and math are on its side. The house doesn't have to be lucky. It just has to keep playing.
-->

---

## The house doesn't need luck.

The gambler does. Time is on the house's side.

<!--
The casino doesn't win every hand — it loses plenty. But the math guarantees that over enough hands, it wins.
So the question isn't "can I win this once," it's "is time working for me or against me."
You want to spend your life on the house side of as many tables as possible.
-->

---

## How do you find "house" games?

The deeper you understand where the world is going, the better you position.

<!--
So how do you actually find games where you're the house? It comes down to understanding reality more deeply than the next person.
What's changing? Where are things heading? Who's going to need what, and when?
The better your read on that, the easier it is to stand on the right side of the table before everyone else figures it out.
-->

---

<!-- _class: statement -->

# Predict the bottleneck. Build the capacity first.

<!--
Here's the concrete move. Look ahead and predict where the bottleneck will be — the thing everyone will scramble for.
Then build that capacity now, calmly, before the scramble starts.
When the rush arrives, you're already the supply. That's how you become the house.
-->

---

## AI is a house game — right now.

The bottleneck: **people who can actually ship with these tools.**

<!--
Live example. AI today is a house game for anyone who learns to build with it early.
The bottleneck isn't the models — it's people who can actually ship real things using them.
Build that skill now, while most people are still just talking about it, and you're positioned as the house when demand explodes.
-->

---

## My rentals: a worked example.

Two properties — **Virginia** and **South Carolina**.

<!--
Let me make it personal with my two rental properties, one in Virginia and one in South Carolina.
I'll walk through them out loud: where's the asymmetry, where's the downside floor, and the honest question —
am I the house here, or am I just a landlord who bought myself a second job? Both can be true; you have to be honest about which.
-->

---

<!-- _class: divider -->

### Part 4
# Build the net before the leap.

<!--
Last part, and it ties the whole thing together.
Everything I've described — the floor, the optionality, the asymmetry — has to be built at a specific time.
And almost nobody builds it then.
-->

---

<!-- _class: statement -->

# Optionality is built in calm weather.

<!--
You build the safety net when the sun is out, not when you're already falling.
I built my runway — savings, skills, fallbacks — over years of calm, boring stability.
That's the only reason today's "risky" move is actually safe. The work was done long before the leap.
-->

---

## The sequence matters.

1. Secure the floor — savings, skills, a fallback
2. *Then* take the swing

<!--
Order of operations. First you cap the downside: build savings, build skills, line up a fallback like Pabrai's old employer.
Only after the floor is in do you take the big swing.
Cap your downside first, and the upside tends to take care of itself. People do this backwards and call the wreckage "bad luck."
-->

---

## Same lesson, two angles.

The personal-finance waterfall and this talk are *one idea*.

<!--
If you sat through the personal-finance waterfall session, this is the same lesson wearing different clothes.
Emergency fund, then debt, then invest — that's "secure the floor, then swing" in money terms.
This talk just generalizes it to time, careers, and bets of every kind.
-->
