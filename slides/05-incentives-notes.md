# Show Me the Incentive — Talking Notes

> One section per slide. Bullets to discuss — talk to the room, don't read them. Slide numbers match the deck.

## Slide 1 — Show Me the Incentive (title)
- Open warm. Tell them today is one mental model, not a hundred facts — but it's the model that reorganizes how you see everything else.
- Promise: "By the end, when you see a bad outcome anywhere — a company, a hospital, your own bad habit — you'll instinctively ask 'what's the incentive?' and you'll usually be right."
- Set the shape: ~45 min of me walking through four real cases, then we play a case-debate game together.
- Name the through-line early: this is about reading systems, not judging individuals.

## Slide 2 — Munger maxim (statement)
- Charlie Munger, Buffett's partner — one of the great worldly-wisdom thinkers. This is his single most quoted line.
- The power of it: it's deterministic. Not "incentives matter a bit" — show me the incentive and I can predict the outcome.
- Once you internalize it, you can't switch it off. You'll see it in pricing, in politics, in your own procrastination.
- Transition: "People are mostly not good or bad — they're mostly responding to what they're rewarded for. Let me prove it four times."

## Slide 3 — Outcomes are the honest report card (statement)
- This is the master key. If they remember one sentence, make it this one.
- The discipline: when results are consistently bad AND the people are smart and well-meaning, STOP blaming the people. Go look at what they're rewarded for.
- Stated intentions are marketing. The outcome is the truth. Believe the outcome over the mission statement every time — foreshadow HCA here.
- This protects you from cynicism too: it lets you assume good faith in people while still being clear-eyed about results.

## Slide 4 — Skin in the game (Taleb)
- Taleb's Skin in the Game runs underneath everything today. The core: the person making the decision should bear the consequences of the decision.
- When the decision-maker is shielded — they keep the upside and hand you the downside — the incentive rots. Think bankers in 2008.
- Give the lens explicitly: at every case today, ask "who eats the consequences here?" The answer predicts the behavior.
- This is why "skin in the game" makes systems honest: consequences re-couple the metric to reality.

## Slide 5 — Case 1 divider: Goodhart's Law
- Transition: "Let's start in my own backyard — AI adoption — because it's the cleanest example I have."
- Tee it up as a cautionary tale: good intentions, a reasonable-sounding metric, and a result nobody wanted.
- Keep energy light here; it's a slightly funny story before the heavier Navy and healthcare cases.

## Slide 6 — Goodhart's Law (statement)
- Goodhart's Law: "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." This is the academic twin of Munger's line.
- Unpack it: a measurement works because it's a faithful proxy for something you care about. The instant you reward the proxy, people game the proxy and the link to the real thing snaps.
- Classic illustrations to mention briefly: teaching to the test, the cobra-breeding bounty, hospital wait-time targets gamed by re-labeling.
- Hold it in mind for the next slide — I'm about to do it to myself.

## Slide 7 — We gamified AI adoption
- The real story: there was a genuine, good push to get people actually using AI tools — adoption was the goal.
- Someone built a leaderboard ranking people by how much they spent on AI tokens — basically "who used it most." Sounds motivating.
- Pause and let them predict it: "What do you think happened?" Often a couple of them call it before I do.

## Slide 8 — Intended vs actual
- The intended outcome was USEFUL AI use. The target that got measured was token SPEND. Those are not the same thing.
- People optimized the metric, not the goal — because the leaderboard rewarded the metric. Rational behavior, bad result.
- Crucial nuance: nobody felt like they were cheating. In their own heads they were "winning the thing we were told to win." That's what makes Goodhart so sneaky.

## Slide 9 — Costs exploded (statement)
- Token spend ballooned. The bill exploded. Activity went up and to the right; value did not follow.
- We literally paid more money to produce a prettier dashboard. The scoreboard looked like success while the actual goal stalled.
- This is Goodhart arriving exactly on schedule — it always shows up; the only question is how fast.

## Slide 10 — The lesson isn't "metrics are bad"
- Be careful not to let them conclude "so never measure anything." You need metrics; you just have to respect them.
- The real lesson: people optimize the EXACT thing you measure, not the thing you meant. Pick carelessly and you get precisely what you asked for and nothing you wanted.
- Land it on them personally: whatever you measure yourself on right now — GPA, follower count, hours "studied" — is shaping your behavior this second. Is that the thing you actually want, or just the thing that's easy to count?

## Slide 11 — Case 2 divider: The Navy FITREP
- Transition: "Now an insider view of a system I lived inside — how the Navy grades its officers."
- Flag your credibility: you were an officer, you were on the receiving end of these, so this is firsthand, not theory.
- Promise a payoff: a clear example of how a system retains certain people and quietly loses others.

## Slide 12 — How a bureaucracy distills a human
- The FITREP — Fitness Report — is the Navy officer's annual evaluation.
- The headline image: a full year of your work, judgment, growth, and character gets compressed into a handful of bullet comments plus numeric grades.
- That compression IS the story. Everything about you that doesn't fit the form effectively didn't happen, as far as the system is concerned.

## Slide 13 — Graded 1.0–5.0 across traits
- For an O-3 (a Lieutenant) you're scored 1.0 to 5.0 across performance traits. Name them deliberately: Professional Expertise; Military Bearing/Character; Teamwork; Mission Accomplishment & Initiative; Leadership — plus Command/Organizational Climate where it applies.
- Get the wording right — this comes from the real instruction (NAVPERS 1610/2, BUPERSINST 1610.7 series).
- Point out: notice what's HARD to score here — quiet mentorship, long-horizon judgment, principled dissent. We'll come back to that.

## Slide 14 — One promotion recommendation
- On top of the trait grades sits a single overall promotion recommendation. Walk the ladder: Significant Problems → Progressing → Promotable → Must Promote (MP) → Early Promote (EP).
- EP is the prize — it's how you get promoted ahead of your peers. Everyone ambitious is chasing EP.
- This one summary mark carries enormous weight on a career — more than any single bullet.

## Slide 15 — Forced distribution (statement)
- Here's the mechanism that changes all the behavior: the top marks, EP and MP, are forced-distribution. Only so many exist per peer group.
- So you're not graded purely on your own merit — you're RANKED against your shipmates. The curve is fixed.
- For you to move up a slot, someone standing next to you moves down. Let that sink in before the next slide.

## Slide 16 — Ranked against your shipmates
- Sit in the human consequence: the people you most need to trust and cooperate with are also your direct competition for the same EP slots.
- It quietly converts a team into a tournament — not because anyone's a bad person, but because the math requires it.
- This is incentive design shaping behavior in real time, inside an institution that genuinely depends on teamwork.

## Slide 17 — What it incentivizes
- Three predictable behaviors. One: competition with peers over collaboration, because the curve is fixed.
- Two: optimizing for what's visible and writable — high-profile, measurable wins you can fit into three bullets. The quiet, important work doesn't write well.
- Three: risk-aversion and conformity. It's hard to look "Early Promote" while taking unconventional swings that might fail — so people stop swinging.

## Slide 18 — What it retains
- The deepest effect isn't on a single year's behavior — it's on WHO stays in the organization over time.
- The system retains people who are excellent at the game it rewards. Good at producing visible, writable wins? You thrive and you stay.
- And it can quietly push out talented non-conformists whose value doesn't fit in three bullets and a number. Over a decade, that reshapes the entire officer corps — you select for the game-players.

## Slide 19 — No system is neutral (statement)
- Be fair and explicit: the FITREP is not evil. It produces exactly the officers it's designed to produce — that's literally its job.
- Generalize it: every evaluation system is a machine that manufactures a certain kind of person. There is no neutral form.
- Give them the action: when you join ANY organization, ask directly — "What does the evaluation form actually reward?" That tells you who you'll become there.

## Slide 20 — Case 3 divider: Politics & Promotion
- Short case, but it reframes something everyone has felt at work.
- Transition: "If a rigid form does this to the Navy, what does the informal version do to a normal company?"

## Slide 21 — "Office politics" isn't a character flaw
- We love labeling a coworker "political" like it's a personality defect. Usually it isn't.
- Politics is a rational response to how advancement is actually structured. If promotions go to the most visible person, people get visible. If they go to whoever the boss likes, people manage the boss.
- Change the promotion criteria and the "politics" change with it — because the behavior is downstream of the incentive, every single time.

## Slide 22 — Read the incentives before you judge
- Practical advice for when they enter the workforce: when behavior frustrates you, don't start with "these people are broken."
- Start with: what does this place reward, promote, and punish? The frustrating behavior usually becomes obvious and even rational.
- Then you get a real choice: play that game knowingly, or go find a different table with incentives you actually want.

## Slide 23 — Case 4 divider: US Healthcare
- This is the most personal case, and I'll tie it to my daughter's recent birth — where I challenged doctors and watched incentives drive recommendations live.
- Set the tone FIRST so nobody mishears me: this is not a conspiracy theory, and doctors are not the villains. It's structure.
- This case is where understanding incentives stops being interesting and starts being protective of your family.

## Slide 24 — Authority meets the income statement
- The structural reality: most hospitals are for-profit institutions operating in a competitive market.
- That's not a scandal in itself — it's just true. The skill is holding two true things at once without collapsing into either "all good" or "all evil."
- Set up the live demo: let me pull up a real, public company's own filings.

## Slide 25 — HCA Healthcare mission
- Pull it up live if you can. HCA Healthcare — a roughly $60 billion-plus publicly traded hospital company.
- Read their 10-K mission statement word for word: "Above all else, we are committed to the care and improvement of human life." Read it slowly — it's genuinely beautiful and I mean that.
- Let it land sincerely before you turn the page. Don't be snide; the sincerity is the point.

## Slide 26 — Accountable to shareholders (statement)
- The very same company is legally accountable to its shareholders to make money. Both statements are true at the same time.
- This isn't hypocrisy — it's a structural tension every for-profit hospital carries by design. Hold both truths at once.
- That tension has to resolve somewhere. It often resolves quietly inside the recommendations individual patients receive.

## Slide 27 — The margin has to come from somewhere
- Hospitals are required to serve Medicare and Medicaid, and under EMTALA they must stabilize any ER patient regardless of ability to pay.
- A lot of that care is a loss leader — reimbursed below cost. That's part of the social contract, and it's real money lost on the income statement.
- Basic arithmetic: to stay profitable, the margin has to come from somewhere else — from the discretionary, well-reimbursed procedures. Hold that thought.

## Slide 28 — The labrum tear
- Concrete example. A partial labrum tear — a shoulder injury — can often be treated two legitimate ways.
- Path one: conservative care — supplements, stretching, a structured rehab routine. Path two: a roughly $50,000 surgery.
- The key sentence: BOTH can be defended medically. There's a genuine gray zone of clinical judgment — and a gray zone is exactly where incentives get room to operate.

## Slide 29 — Authority bias (Cialdini)
- Now add authority bias, from Cialdini's Influence. Humans are wired to defer to credentialed experts, and the white coat is the strongest authority cue there is.
- The claim is measured: there's a NON-ZERO chance the institution's need for revenue subtly tilts the recommendation toward the surgery. Not always — non-zero.
- The subtle, important part: a sincere doctor may not even feel the tilt. Incentives shape judgment below conscious awareness. This isn't about lying — it's about being human inside a system.

## Slide 30 — The point is not "doctors are bad" (statement)
- Say it plainly and mean it: doctors went to school to help people. They'll tell you what they genuinely believe is best for you.
- But "best" can be quietly colored by the incentive structure around them. That's not corruption — it's the water they swim in.
- The real problem is asymmetry: many patients see through it, many don't. Knowing the incentives just moves you into the first group.

## Slide 31 — Better questions
- The payoff of all this isn't cynicism — it's better questions. Cynicism makes you a worse patient; good questions make you a better one.
- Two to use: "What would you do if it were YOUR shoulder?" and "What's the most conservative path we could try first?"
- These don't accuse anyone. They gently realign the conversation around your interests instead of the institution's — and a good doctor will respect them.

## Slide 32 — My daughter's birth
- Make it real and human: during my daughter's recent birth, I found myself asking exactly these questions and challenging some default recommendations.
- I could watch in real time how the default path and the incentive-shaped path weren't always identical — and where to push gently.
- I'm not anti-doctor — I'm pro-asking. Becoming a father raised the stakes on this skill overnight, and understanding incentives let me advocate for my family well.

## Slide 33 — Part 5 divider: Turn the lens on yourself
- The pivot: "We've audited the Navy, corporations, and hospitals. Now the uncomfortable part — let's point the same lens at you."
- This is where analysis becomes a tool they can use tonight, not just an interesting talk.

## Slide 34 — You will become what you incentivize (statement)
- The same law that governs the Navy and HCA governs you. Whatever you reward yourself for, you get more of. Whatever you measure, you optimize — consciously or not.
- So the question is never "am I being shaped by incentives?" You are, right now, by default. The only question is whether you chose them or inherited them by accident.

## Slide 35 — Design your own incentives on purpose
- Three levers they actually control. What you REWARD yourself for — celebrate the right behaviors (showing up, shipping), not just lucky outcomes.
- Who you're AROUND — you absorb the incentives of your environment, so choose the room deliberately; your peer group is a forced distribution too.
- What you MEASURE — pick metrics pointed at the life you actually want, not the easiest proxy. Design these like you'd design any system: on purpose.

## Slide 36 — Show me YOUR incentive (statement)
- Bring Munger full circle, now aimed at them: "Show me your incentive and I'll show you your outcome."
- If they want to predict their own future, don't look at their stated goals — look at what they're actually rewarded for, day to day.
- The empowering twist: that report card is honest, and unlike the FITREP, they get to design it.

## Slide 37 — Case debate setup
- Before Q&A, play a game so they practice reading incentives FORWARD, before outcomes arrive.
- Give a scenario — e.g., a startup's sales-commission plan, or a school's grading policy — and have the room predict the behavior it'll produce.
- Coach them to trace it: who's measured on what, who bears the consequences, what gets gamed. Then open the floor to anything.

## Slide 38 — Close (lead)
- Land the master key one last time: when outcomes are bad and the people are smart and sincere, examine the incentives, not the individuals.
- Then ask them to turn that same generosity and rigor inward — design the incentives that will design them.
- Thank them, take questions. For the curious, the books underneath today: Skin in the Game (Taleb), Influence (Cialdini), and Munger's incentive maxim in the spirit of Poor Charlie's Almanack.
