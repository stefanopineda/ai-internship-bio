# Show Me the Incentive

## I'll show you the outcome

<!--
Welcome them. Today is about a single mental model that, once you see it, you can't unsee — in companies, in government, in your doctor's office, in your own habits.
The promise: by the end you'll be able to look at any bad outcome and trace it back to the incentive that produced it.
This is Incentives 101 — the hidden force behind every behavior. ~45 min of talking, then a case debate.
-->

---

<!-- _class: statement -->

> Show me the incentive and I'll show you the outcome.

### — Charlie Munger

<!--
Charlie Munger, Warren Buffett's partner, said this — and it's the master key for the whole session.
Once you can read incentive structures, you can't unsee them. It changes how you walk through the world.
Say it plainly: people aren't mostly good or bad — they're mostly responding to what they're rewarded for.
-->

---

<!-- _class: statement -->

# Outcomes are the honest report card.

<!--
This is the single idea I most want them to leave with.
When results are consistently bad — and smart, well-meaning people keep producing them — stop blaming the people. Examine what they're being rewarded for.
The outcome is the truth. The stated intention is just marketing. Believe the outcome.
-->

---

## Underneath all of it: skin in the game

> People behave differently when they bear the consequences of their own decisions.

*Nassim Taleb — Skin in the Game*

<!--
Taleb's idea runs beneath everything today: the person making the call should bear the downside of the call.
When the decision-maker is shielded from consequences, the incentive rots — they capture the upside and pass you the risk.
Keep this lens handy as we walk through four real cases. Ask each time: who eats the consequences?
-->

### Case 1

# Goodhart's Law

<!--
Our first case is the cleanest illustration of the whole principle, and it comes from my own world: AI adoption.
Set it up as a cautionary tale about measuring the wrong thing.
-->

---

<!-- _class: statement -->

> When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure.

### — Goodhart's Law

<!--
This is Goodhart's Law. It's the technical version of Munger's maxim.
The moment you turn a measurement into a goal, people start optimizing the measurement itself — and it stops telling you the truth.
Hold this in your head for the next story.
-->

---

## We gamified AI adoption.

**A leaderboard ranking who spent the most on AI tokens.**

<!--
Real example I'll narrate. A push to drive AI adoption — get people actually using these tools.
Someone built a leaderboard ranking people by token spend: who used the AI the most.
Intent was good: more adoption, more useful work. Watch what actually happened.
-->

---

## Intended: adoption. Actual: a metric.

- The goal was *useful* AI use
- The target became *token spend*
- People optimized the **metric**, not the **goal**

<!--
The intended outcome was useful adoption. The actual outcome was people optimizing the number on the board.
Token spend is a proxy for value — and the moment it became the target, the proxy and the goal divorced.
Nobody was cheating in their own mind. They were doing exactly what the scoreboard rewarded.
-->

---

<!-- _class: statement -->

# Token spend ballooned. Costs exploded.

<!--
The numbers went up and to the right — and so did the bill.
Activity went up; value did not. We paid more to get a prettier dashboard.
This is Goodhart's Law arriving on schedule. It always does.
-->

---

## The lesson isn't "metrics are bad."

**People optimize the *exact* thing you measure — not the thing you meant.**

<!--
The takeaway is not that metrics are evil — you need them. It's that you'll get precisely what you measure and nothing you only intended.
Pick the metric carelessly and you'll get exactly what you asked for and nothing you wanted.
Whatever YOU measure yourself on right now — GPA, follower count, hours studied — is shaping you this second. Is it the thing you actually want?
-->

### Case 2

# The Navy FITREP

<!--
Now an insider breakdown — how the Navy incentivizes its officers, and what that produces.
This is the system I lived inside as an officer, so I can speak to it honestly.
-->

---

## How a bureaucracy distills a human

**One year of your work →**
a handful of bullets + numbers.

<!--
The FITREP — Fitness Report — is how the Navy evaluates an officer.
A full year of your work, judgment, and growth gets compressed into a handful of bullet comments and a set of numeric grades.
That compression is the whole story. Everything that doesn't fit gets lost.
-->

---

## An O-3 FITREP: graded 1.0–5.0

- Professional Expertise
- Military Bearing / Character
- Teamwork
- Mission Accomplishment & Initiative
- Leadership

<!--
For an O-3 — a Lieutenant — you're graded 1.0 to 5.0 across performance traits.
Walk them slowly: Professional Expertise, Military Bearing/Character, Teamwork, Mission Accomplishment & Initiative, Leadership — plus Command/Organizational Climate where it applies.
Five traits and a number try to capture a whole human being's year. Notice already what won't fit in there.
-->

---

## One promotion recommendation

Significant Problems → Progressing → Promotable → **Must Promote** → **Early Promote**

<!--
On top of the traits sits a single promotion recommendation ranking.
The ladder: Significant Problems, Progressing, Promotable, Must Promote (MP), Early Promote (EP).
EP is the top mark — the one that gets you ahead of your peers. Everyone is chasing it.
-->

---

<!-- _class: statement -->

# The top marks are forced-distribution.

<!--
Here's the twist that changes everyone's behavior: EP and MP are forced-distribution.
Only so many exist per peer group. You aren't just graded on your own merit — you're ranked against your shipmates.
The curve is fixed. For you to go up, someone next to you goes down.
-->

---

## You're ranked against your shipmates.

*The curve is fixed. Your peers are your competition.*

<!--
Sit with what a fixed curve does to a team. The people you most need to cooperate with are also the people you're competing against for the same slots.
It quietly converts collaboration into competition — not because anyone is bad, but because the math demands it.
This is incentive design shaping behavior in real time.
-->

---

## What it incentivizes

- Competition over collaboration
- The **visible and writable** — wins that fit three bullets
- **Risk-aversion** and conformity

<!--
So what does this structure reward? Competing with peers over collaborating with them, because of the curve.
Optimizing for what's visible and writable — high-profile, measurable wins you can put in three bullets.
And a strong nudge toward risk-aversion and conformity: it's hard to look "Early Promote" while taking unconventional swings that might fail.
-->

---

## What it retains

**It keeps the people who excel at the game it rewards —**

*and quietly pushes out talented non-conformists.*

<!--
Now the retention effect, which is the deepest part. A system doesn't just rank people; it decides who stays.
The FITREP retains people who are excellent at the game it rewards.
And it can quietly push out talented non-conformists whose value doesn't fit in three bullets and a number. You lose exactly the people who color outside the lines.
-->

---

<!-- _class: statement -->

# No system is neutral.

<!--
The FITREP isn't evil. It produces exactly the officers it's designed to produce — that's the point.
Every evaluation system is a machine that manufactures a certain kind of person.
So when you join any organization, ask the question directly: what does the evaluation form actually reward?
-->

### Case 3

# Politics & Promotion

<!--
Quick but important case — why good people do frustrating things at work.
This reframes "office politics" from a character flaw into an incentive response.
-->

---

## "Office politics" isn't a character flaw.

**It's a rational response to how advancement is structured.**

<!--
We love to call coworkers "political" like it's a personality defect. It usually isn't.
Politics is a rational response to how advancement is actually structured. Change the promotion criteria and the "politics" change with it.
The behavior is downstream of the incentive — every time.
-->

---

## Read the incentives before you judge.

*When you join a company, read the structure before you judge the behavior.*

<!--
Practical advice: when you join a company and see behavior that frustrates you, don't start with "these people are broken."
Start with: what does this place reward, promote, and punish? The behavior will make sense fast.
Then you can decide whether you want to play that game or find a different table.
-->

### Case 4

# US Healthcare

<!--
The most personal example. I'll tie it to my daughter's recent birth, where I challenged doctors and watched incentives drive recommendations in real time.
Frame carefully up front: this is not a conspiracy and doctors are not villains. It's structure.
-->

---

## Authority meets the income statement

*Not a conspiracy. Just incentives.*

<!--
The setup: most hospitals are for-profit institutions operating in a market.
That's not a scandal — it's the structural reality. The job is to hold two true things at once without flinching.
Let me pull up a real, public example.
-->

---

## HCA Healthcare — a ~$60B public company

> "Above all else, we are committed to the care and improvement of human life."

*— HCA Healthcare, mission statement (10-K)*

<!--
Pull this up live if you can. HCA Healthcare — a roughly $60 billion-plus publicly traded hospital company.
Their 10-K mission statement reads, word for word: "Above all else, we are committed to the care and improvement of human life." It's genuinely beautiful.
Read it aloud, let it land, then turn the page.
-->

---

<!-- _class: statement -->

# And it is legally accountable to shareholders.

<!--
The same company is legally accountable to its shareholders to make money. Both are true.
This isn't hypocrisy — it's a built-in tension every for-profit hospital carries. Hold both truths at once.
That tension has to resolve somewhere, and it resolves in the recommendations patients receive.
-->

---

## The margin has to come from somewhere.

- Required to serve **Medicare / Medicaid**
- Must stabilize ER patients (**EMTALA**)
- Often **loss leaders** — reimbursed below cost

<!--
Hospitals are required to serve Medicare and Medicaid, and to stabilize any ER patient under EMTALA — regardless of ability to pay.
A lot of that care is a loss leader, reimbursed below cost. That's a feature of the social contract, and it's real money lost.
To stay profitable, the margin has to come from somewhere else. Keep that pressure in mind for the next slide.
-->

---

## The labrum tear

**Conservative rehab** — supplements, stretching, structured routine
**— or —**
**~$50,000 surgery**

*Both can be defended medically.*

<!--
Concrete example. A partial labrum tear — a shoulder injury — can often be treated two ways.
One: conservative care — supplements, stretching, a structured rehab routine. Two: a roughly $50,000 surgery.
Here's the key: both paths can be defended medically. There's a genuine judgment-call range, and that's exactly where incentives get to operate.
-->

---

## Now add authority bias

*The person recommending wears the white coat.*

**— Robert Cialdini, Influence**

<!--
Now layer in authority bias — Cialdini, from Influence. We're wired to defer to credentialed experts, and a white coat is the ultimate authority cue.
There's a non-zero chance the institution's need for revenue subtly tilts the recommendation toward the surgery.
And here's the subtle part — a sincere doctor may not even feel the tilt. Incentives shape judgment below the level of conscious intent.
-->

---

<!-- _class: statement -->

# The point is not "doctors are bad."

<!--
Say this clearly and mean it. Doctors went to school to help people. They'll tell you what they genuinely believe is best.
But "best" can be quietly colored by the incentive structure around them. That's not corruption — it's being human inside a system.
Many patients see through it; many don't. The asymmetry of who-knows is the real problem.
-->

---

## Knowing the incentives = better questions

- *"What would you do if it were your shoulder?"*
- *"What's the conservative path first?"*

<!--
The payoff of understanding incentives isn't cynicism — it's better questions.
Ask: "What would you do if it were your own shoulder?" and "What's the most conservative path we could try first?"
These questions don't accuse anyone. They just realign the conversation with your interests instead of the institution's.
-->

---

## My daughter's birth

*I challenged doctors and watched incentives drive recommendations in real time.*

<!--
This got personal recently. During my daughter's birth, I found myself challenging recommendations and asking exactly these questions.
I could watch, in real time, how the default path and the incentive-shaped path weren't always the same thing.
Becoming a father raised the stakes on this skill overnight. I'm not anti-doctor — I'm pro-asking. Knowing the incentives let me advocate for my family.
-->

### Part 5

# Turn the Lens on Yourself

<!--
We've audited the Navy, corporations, and hospitals. Now the uncomfortable turn — point the same lens inward.
This is where the talk stops being analysis and becomes a tool they can use tonight.
-->

---

<!-- _class: statement -->

# You will become what you incentivize.

<!--
The same law that governs the Navy and HCA governs you. Whatever you reward yourself for, you'll get more of.
Whatever you measure, you'll optimize — consciously or not.
So the question isn't whether you're being shaped by incentives. You are. The question is whether you chose them.
-->

---

## Design your own incentives on purpose.

- What you **reward** yourself for
- Who you're **around**
- What you **measure**

<!--
Three levers you actually control. What you reward yourself for — celebrate the right behaviors, not just outcomes.
Who you're around — you absorb the incentives of your environment, so choose the room.
And what you measure — pick metrics that point at the life you want, not the easy proxy. Design these on purpose, the way you'd design any system.
-->

---

<!-- _class: statement -->

> Show me YOUR incentive — and I'll show you your outcome.

<!--
Bring it full circle to Munger, now pointed at them.
If they want to predict their own future, don't look at their goals — look at what they're actually rewarded for day to day.
That's the honest report card, and they get to design it.
-->

---

Then open floor for anything — companies, government, your doctor, your own habits.
