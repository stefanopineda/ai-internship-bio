# You Don't Rise to the Occasion — Talking Notes

> One section per slide. Bullets to discuss — talk to the room, don't read them. Slide numbers match the deck.

## Slide 1 — You Don't Rise to the Occasion (title)
- Open with where you've been: years on a submarine, an environment where the cost of getting it wrong is everyone's life.
- State the promise of the talk: I'm going to kill a myth about heroes and crises, then hand you a way to perform under any pressure.
- Set expectations: ~45 min of talk, then 15 of Q&A; questions welcome at the end.
- Reminder to self: all submarine content stays at the unclassified engineering/seamanship level — buoyancy, ship systems, human performance. No tactics.

## Slide 2 — Freezing isn't embarrassing here. It's fatal.
- On a sub, not knowing the procedure or freezing up isn't an awkward moment — it can get everyone aboard killed.
- That stakes-level is a gift: it strips away vanity and forces an honest answer to "what is leadership actually for?"
- Everything I learned was forged here, but I'll bring it all the way down to your interview, your pitch, your first hard conversation with a co-founder.
- Transition: so let's start with the lie we've all been sold about moments like these.

## Slide 3 — The myth: in a crisis, heroes rise.
- The cultural story: when the alarm sounds, the hero finds another gear and rises to the occasion.
- It's cinematic and it's wrong. Great movie, terrible operating model.
- I've been in real crises. That extra gear does not appear on command — and betting your life on it is reckless.
- Transition: here's what actually happens instead.

## Slide 4 — You don't rise to the occasion. You fall to your level of preparation. (thesis)
- This is the whole talk in one sentence. The military phrasing I lived by: "you do not rise to the occasion; you fall to your level of training."
- Mechanism: under pressure you do not get smarter. You execute whatever you've already burned into muscle memory.
- So the leader's entire job is to raise that level in advance — in the calm, before the alarm.
- Say it twice and let it land. Everything after this is just "how."

## Slide 5 — Calm is not a personality trait.
- People watch a calm operator and assume temperament — "they're just wired that way."
- No. Calm is a product of preparation. It's manufactured beforehand through reps, not summoned in the moment.
- You don't get to be calmer than your preparation made you. The composure you'll show is already set before the event.
- Transition: so where does that preparation actually come from? It's boring.

## Slide 6 — Part 1: Preparation is the performance (divider)
- Reframe the whole first act: the performance you admire isn't the crisis — it's the preparation, showing up later.
- Tell them this section is deliberately unglamorous, and that's the point.
- Set up the drills story.

## Slide 7 — Countless drills. On purpose boring.
- I ran countless drills in simulators — steam-line ruptures, casualties of every kind — over and over until the response was automatic.
- The drills are boring on purpose. Boring means it's turning into reflex instead of a decision you have to make while scared.
- The whole design: absorb the drama in the simulator so there's none left to manufacture courage for in the real event.
- Repetition is not a punishment here; it's the mechanism that builds the floor.

## Slide 8 — Performance = (Preparation × Knowledge), executed calmly.
- Give them the equation: Performance under pressure = (Preparation × Knowledge) executed calmly.
- It's a product, not a sum — if either factor is near zero, the whole thing collapses no matter how brave you feel.
- "Executed calmly" isn't a separate skill; from slide 5, calm is itself the output of the first two factors.
- Takeaway: you raise your ceiling under pressure by raising either factor in the calm, in advance.

## Slide 9 — "Coming in clutch" is just a high floor.
- From the outside, clutch looks like talent or courage. From the inside it's unglamorous: a high floor.
- You prepared so thoroughly that even your worst, most-stressed self still clears the bar.
- The reframe: you're not betting on your best day showing up — you're guaranteeing your bad day is good enough.
- This is the most freeing idea in the talk: you don't need to be a hero, you need a high floor.

## Slide 10 — When did you crack? When did you nail it? (audience prompt)
- Ask the room to think of one time they cracked under pressure and one time they nailed it.
- Then the real question: what was actually different between the two?
- Let a couple of people answer out loud if the room is warm.
- Land it: almost every time the difference is preparation, not courage or mood. You'd done the reps, or you hadn't.

## Slide 11 — Part 2: Sea stories (divider)
- Transition into four real engineering casualties from the boat.
- Tell them each was decided before the alarm, by training — listen for that beat in each story.
- Reminder to self: keep every one of these at the unclassified engineering/seamanship level — buoyancy, fluid systems, valve failures, human response. No tactics. When in doubt, leave it out.

## Slide 12 — Every outcome was decided before the alarm.
- Frame all four: each told at the high engineering level only — buoyancy, fluid systems, valve failures, human response.
- The repeating point: the "success" moment is just preparation cashing out. The work was already done.
- Prime them to spot it in each story so the lesson compounds.
- Reminder: keep everything unclassified — engineering and human performance only.

## Slide 13 — Sea story 1: Hydraulic rupture (divider)
- Set the scene: a high-pressure hydraulic system lets go — sudden, loud, high-energy.
- Convey the visceral stakes without any operational detail.
- Reminder to self: unclassified engineering level only — fluid systems and human response, nothing operational.

## Slide 14 — Isolate. Control. Communicate.
- The trained response to a high-pressure rupture: isolate the source, control the affected system, communicate clearly across the team.
- Why it beats improvisation every time: someone already thought it through when nobody was bleeding adrenaline; in the moment you just execute it.
- The win wasn't a hero finding genius — it was people doing the boring sequence fast and in order.
- Lesson: isolate / control / communicate is a portable pattern for any sudden failure, on a boat or in a business.
- Reminder: keep this at the fluid-systems and human-performance level only.

## Slide 15 — Sea story 2: Refrigerant leak (divider)
- Set the scene: a refrigerant leak in a confined space — a genuine hazard to the people in the compartment.
- Emphasize it's a human-safety event, not an abstract systems one.
- Reminder to self: unclassified — confined-space hazard and human response only.

## Slide 16 — Confined space. Calm procedure wins.
- The hazard is real: refrigerant in a confined space threatens the people in it.
- But panic is the larger hazard — it's what turns a manageable event into a casualty.
- Trained procedure (protect people, ventilate, follow the steps) converts a scary moment into a controlled one, and that calm came from drilling it cold.
- Lesson repeats from slide 5: calm procedure beats fast panic, and the calm was pre-loaded.
- Reminder: keep this at the confined-space-hazard and human-performance level only.

## Slide 17 — Sea story 3: Reverse-osmosis repairs (divider)
- Set the scene: the water plant — reverse osmosis, the system that makes the crew's fresh water.
- Flag the tone shift: this one has no alarm and no movie moment.
- Reminder to self: unclassified — just reliability and maintenance of a critical system.

## Slide 18 — Reliability is a leadership outcome.
- The reverse-osmosis plant is the opposite of a dramatic casualty: the daily, unglamorous grind of keeping critical equipment alive.
- No alarm, no heroics — just relentless maintenance that nobody applauds.
- But reliability is a leadership outcome you manufacture with exactly that grind; the absence of crisis is the product.
- Lesson: most of leadership isn't the crisis — it's the boring upkeep that prevents the crisis from ever happening.
- Reminder: keep this at the maintenance/reliability level only.

## Slide 19 — Sea story 4: Loss of depth control (divider)
- Set up the most important story: the one real casualty I personally had to manage — not a drill.
- Raise the gravity in the room before you tell it.
- Critical reminder to self: tell this strictly at the physics/seamanship level — buoyancy and remotely-operated-valve failure behavior. No tactics, no operational specifics. When in doubt, leave it out.

## Slide 20 — Buoyancy. A valve that should hold — didn't.
- At the unclassified level: this is fundamentally about buoyancy and the failure behavior of remotely-operated valves.
- When a system that should hold doesn't, the boat's control of its own depth is at stake — that's the physics, and I keep it there.
- Stress that this was real, not a simulation, and it was mine to manage.
- Reminder to self: physics and seamanship only — buoyancy and ROV failure modes. No tactics, no specifics.

## Slide 21 — What made it go right was not heroics.
- There was no hero moment. What made it go right was people falling back on trained procedure and communicating clearly.
- The training absorbed the drama; we executed what we'd already burned in.
- This is the thesis proven in the highest-stakes case I personally faced: we didn't rise — we fell to our level of preparation, and the floor was high enough.
- Drive it home: that floor existed because of countless boring drills nobody enjoyed.
- Reminder: keep it at the buoyancy / ROV-failure / human-performance level.

## Slide 22 — The drama is absorbed by the training.
- The common thread across all four: every time it went right, it was because people performed the preparation.
- The training soaked up the drama so the humans didn't have to manufacture courage on the spot.
- That's the entire case for drilling the boring stuff in advance.
- Transition: now let's name a distinction that decides whether your preparation even points at the right things — leading vs. managing.

## Slide 23 — Part 3: Leading vs. Managing (divider)
- Tell them most people blur these two and end up doing neither well.
- Both matter enormously. They are simply not the same job.
- Set up the two definitions coming next.

## Slide 24 — Managing = systems, procedures, the known.
- Management is about systems, procedures, and the known — keeping the trains running, the checklist complete, the standard enforced.
- This is essential: the submarine runs on superb management; without it the boat doesn't float.
- Don't let anyone frame management as the lesser skill — it's just a different skill from leadership.

## Slide 25 — Leading = people, the unknown.
- Leadership is about people and the unknown — setting direction, absorbing fear so the team can function, making the call with incomplete information, owning it when it goes wrong.
- Highlight "absorbing fear": in a casualty the leader's job is to keep humans from freezing while management keeps the procedure exact.
- The line to say out loud: you're not managing the valve, you're leading the person turning it.

## Slide 26 — In a casualty, you need both at once.
- You don't pick one — you switch between leading and managing constantly, sometimes second to second.
- In a casualty: management keeps the procedure exact; leadership keeps the humans executing it from freezing.
- Great operators toggle so fast it looks like one thing, but it's two, and naming them lets you get better at each.

## Slide 27 — Covey: right things vs. things right.
- Covey: management is doing things right; leadership is doing the right things.
- The image: management is the speed and form of climbing the ladder; leadership is making sure the ladder leans against the right wall.
- Warning: you can be flawlessly efficient climbing the wrong wall — that's why you need both, not a favorite.
- Transition: now I'll take this exact distinction off the boat and into a startup.

## Slide 28 — Part 4: Delegation vs. Abdication (divider)
- Transplant the same lesson from the submarine to building a company.
- Frame it through Gerber's The E-Myth Revisited.
- Promise a clean, practical distinction founders constantly get wrong.

## Slide 29 — E-Myth: work ON it, not IN it.
- Gerber's core insight: most small businesses fail because the founder works in the business — the technician doing the task — instead of on it, building the system that does the task.
- On a sub you cannot personally do every job; you build the system that does them. Same with a company.
- The technician trap feels productive and quietly kills the business, because nobody is building the machine.

## Slide 30 — Delegation = system + standard + accountability.
- Delegation is handing off a task with a clear system, a clear standard, and accountability — then verifying.
- The leader still owns the outcome, always: you delegate the task, you never delegate the ownership.
- The verification step is the whole difference between trusting blindly and trusting safely.

## Slide 31 — Abdication = throw it over the wall and hope.
- Abdication is throwing a task over the wall and hoping — no system, no standard, no follow-up.
- This is how new founders get burned and then wrongly conclude "I have to do everything myself."
- Diagnosis matters: they didn't delegate and fail, they abdicated — and the cure is different.

## Slide 32 — "I have to do everything myself" is a symptom of abdication.
- When a founder says this, I don't hear a personality trait — I hear a broken process.
- They handed off tasks with no system, no standard, no follow-up, and got predictably burned.
- The fix isn't to take everything back; it's to build the system that makes handing off safe — exactly what the boat forced on me.

## Slide 33 — The boat taught me delegation at gunpoint.
- The submarine taught me delegation at gunpoint: I had to trust trained watchstanders to execute under real risk.
- But the trust wasn't faith — I built and verified the system that made it safe: the drills, the procedures, the qualifications.
- You delegate the task; you never abdicate the ownership. That's exactly what a founder must learn, just with lower stakes and more patience.

## Slide 34 — Delegate the task. Never abdicate the ownership.
- The single line to remember from the whole business section.
- Same idea on the boat and in the startup: hand off the doing, keep the owning.
- If it breaks, it's yours — and that's not a burden, that's the definition of the job.
- Transition: so how do you build all of this for yourself, starting this week?

## Slide 35 — Part 5: Build your own high floor (divider)
- Bring it home for people who'll never touch a submarine.
- Same mechanism, much lower stakes: you build your own high floor.
- Set up the two concrete moves coming next.

## Slide 36 — Pick the high-stakes moments. Drill them.
- Pick the situations you already know will be high-stakes: an interview, a pitch, a hard conversation.
- Drill them before they happen — rehearse out loud, anticipate the casualty (the tough question, the objection, the moment it goes sideways).
- You're literally running the simulator for your own life; the boring reps are the point, same as the drills.

## Slide 37 — Raise the floor in the calm.
- The whole move is timing: raise your knowledge and preparation in the calm times.
- Then when pressure hits, your stressed self inherits a floor it didn't have to build under fire.
- Repeat the mechanism: you will not be smarter in the moment, so get smarter before the moment — every time.

## Slide 38 — Don't try to be a hero. Build a higher floor.
- The reframe I want them to walk out with: stop waiting to rise, stop trying to be the hero in the moment.
- Build the floor so high that even your worst day clears the bar.
- This isn't less ambitious than heroism — it's how heroism actually gets manufactured, in advance, in the calm.

- The exercise: name one high-pressure event coming up in your life, then design the "drill" that would raise your floor for it — rehearse out loud, anticipate the casualty, run it before it's real.
