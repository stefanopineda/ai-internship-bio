# I Knocked on 1000 Doors — Talking Notes

> One section per slide. Bullets to discuss — talk to the room, don't read them. Slide numbers match the deck.

## Slide 1 — Title
- Open warm. "Quick show of hands — who here has ever talked their way out of a speeding ticket, or talked their parents into something? Congratulations, you've already closed a sale."
- Set the frame: the *medium* of selling has changed completely in 30 years; the *human* on the other side hasn't changed at all.
- Promise: by the end you'll have a framework for getting customers and the psychology that makes people say yes — useful whether or not you ever start a company.

## Slide 2 — Everyone is always selling something
- Sales is the most underrated skill in entrepreneurship, full stop.
- The brutal truth: most technical founders can build anything and sell nothing. Their company dies with a beautiful product nobody bought.
- "If you can build but can't sell, you're dependent on someone who can. If you can sell, you'll never starve." It's the most transferable skill there is.

## Slide 3 — You're always selling you
- Read the room honestly: "I know not everyone here wants to do a startup. That's totally fine — this still applies to every single one of you."
- An **interview is a sales call**: you are the product, and you're persuading them to "buy" you over other candidates.
- Two paths, same skill: a **founder** sells a vision to customers and investors; an **intrapreneur** sells ideas and projects to a boss inside a company. Both live or die on persuasion.
- Land it: "You will spend your whole life selling yourself. Might as well get good at it on purpose."

## Slide 4 — I volunteered to knock on doors
- The confession: between submarine assignments I *volunteered* for two months of door-to-door Verizon 5G sales. Didn't need the money.
- Why: I wanted the reps. Engineers and military officers aren't "natural salespeople" — I wanted to build the muscle deliberately.
- Point for them: skills like this are *trainable*. You're not born with it. You go get the reps.

## Slide 5 — 50 no's, ring the next bell
- The real lesson of the doorstep is emotional: **detachment from outcome.**
- You hear "no" far more than "yes." If each no wounds you, you quit by lunch. If you treat each no as a data point, you win.
- This is the emotional core of all sales AND all entrepreneurship: volume plus resilience. The people who win are the ones who can eat rejection and keep moving.

## Slide 6 — Part 1: The evolution of the sale
- Transition: "Let's zoom out. Selling has gone through four eras. Watch what changes — and what never does."

## Slide 7 — Era 1: The marketplace
- The bazaar, the general store, the handshake. Selling in person, face to face.
- Sam Walton (*Made in America*) built the world's biggest retailer by walking his own store floors and obsessing over customers.
- The lesson that survives every era: relentless customer obsession. Go where the customer is and *watch* them.

## Slide 8 — Era 2: Door-to-door
- My Verizon stint lives here. You're interrupting someone's dinner — you have about 7 seconds.
- Tonality, posture, and your very first sentence decide everything.
- Big takeaway: *how* you say it beats *what* you say. People buy your energy before they buy your offer.

## Slide 9 — Era 3: The phone
- Phone sales: volume, scripts, objection handling at scale.
- Not ancient history for me — I cold-call small businesses *today* for my AI receptionist startup.
- The phone removed the walk between doors. Same game, far faster.

## Slide 10 — Era 4: Digital
- Landing pages, email, DMs, ads. The doorbell became a "Notify me" button.
- You can now knock on 10,000 doors while you sleep — leverage that simply didn't exist before.
- And it's available to a 19-year-old with a laptop. That's genuinely new in human history.

## Slide 11 — The channel changed, the human didn't
- The thesis of the whole talk. Say it slowly.
- Every era rewards exactly two things: **earning attention** and **reducing the friction to "yes."**
- Master the human and you're future-proof — it doesn't matter what the next channel (AI agents, whatever) turns out to be.

## Slide 12 — Part 2: How you take money & measure demand
- Transition: "Enough history. Here's what you can literally do this week to turn an idea into a business."

## Slide 13 — Accept payment before you build
- The biggest beginner mistake: build for six months in a cave, *then* try to sell.
- Flip it. A single Stripe link or pre-order page first. If people pay, you have a business. If they don't, you just saved six months of your life.

## Slide 14 — If they won't pay, it's a hobby
- Harsh but freeing. "I'd totally buy that" from a friend is worth nothing.
- A credit card number is truth. Money is the only honest vote.
- Encourage them to find the cheapest way to ask for money early.

## Slide 15 — "Be the first to find out"
- The cheapest market test on earth: a one-page site that says "Be the first to know when it goes live," with an email box.
- Real emails from strangers massively outweigh polite encouragement from people who love you.
- You can validate an idea this weekend for $0, before writing a single line of product.

## Slide 16 — Pre-sells beat surveys
- Surveys lie, because saying "yes" on a survey is free. A pre-order costs the customer something, so it tells the truth.
- Tesla took **$5,000 deposits** for a Roadster that didn't exist yet — and did the same playbook with the Cybertruck and the Semi.
- Facebook made you wait in line for an invite (scarcity + a demand signal). You can pre-order a book before it's even printed.
- The pattern: collect real money or real commitment *before* you build. Tie to *Ready, Fire, Aim* (Masterson) — get something real in front of customers fast.
- (If the screenshot is in: point at the "$5,000 due today" — "for a car that didn't exist.")

## Slide 17 — Part 3: The Hormozi map
- Transition: "Okay, you've got something to sell. How do you actually get customers? Hormozi says there are exactly four ways. Just four."

## Slide 18 — Four lanes, only four
- Two questions define every lane: Do they already know you? (warm vs cold) Are you reaching one or many? (1-to-1 vs 1-to-many)
- That's the whole grid: warm outreach, cold outreach, content, paid ads. Everything you've ever seen is one of these four.

## Slide 19 — Warm outreach
- Texting and calling people who already know you. Your "100-person list."
- It's free and it converts best — which is why it's where almost everyone should start.
- Why people skip it: selling to people you know feels uncomfortable. Do it anyway.

## Slide 20 — Cold outreach
- Cold calls, cold DMs, cold email to strangers. This is my daily reality right now.
- The formula: volume × personalization × follow-up.
- The money is in the follow-up — most people quit after one touch, so the few who follow up 5–7 times win.

## Slide 21 — Content
- One-to-many, warm. Posting free, useful content that builds an audience over time.
- I made a YouTube video about a tool I built — it keeps selling my work while I sleep. That's compounding leverage.
- Slower to start, but it stacks; outreach stops the moment you stop sending.

## Slide 22 — Paid ads
- One-to-many, cold. Renting attention with money.
- Critical: only turn this on *after* your offer already converts organically.
- Ads pour fuel on a fire. If there's no fire, you just burn cash. Most beginners do this backwards.

## Slide 23 — Saturate one lane first
- The beginner death is doing all four lanes badly at once.
- Pick ONE lane, go deep until it's working and predictable, *then* add the next.
- Focus beats diversification when you're small. (Echoes *The Ultimate Sales Machine* — pigheaded discipline on a few things.)

## Slide 24 — Part 4: The psychology that never changes (Influence)
- Transition: "Channels change. The brain doesn't. Everything from here is 40+ years old and still runs the show."
- Hold up *Influence* by Robert Cialdini — "if you read one book after today, read this one."
- Note: I'm going to give you these in the order I personally memorize them.

## Slide 25 — Reciprocity 🤝
- Give first. When you provide value up front — a free teardown, a genuinely useful tip — people feel a pull to give back.
- This is *why* "free value" outreach beats "buy my thing" outreach.
- Practical: lead with something useful before you ever ask.

## Slide 26 — Liking 😊  (the doorstep story)
- People buy from people they like. The fastest way to be liked: bring light to someone's day.
- **The story:** I hit a long streak where people slammed me with "no, not interested" before I could even start talking. So I went off-script. *Before* saying anything about myself, I'd compliment something — their house, their car, their garden.
- Even an ugly, unmaintained house: I'd say, "I love the color of these bricks — looks really sturdy and earthy." The tone flips *instantly*. The moment you give a genuine compliment, the door stays open.
- **The picture:** imagine two fruit kiosks side by side — one clerk smiling, one frowning. You buy from the smiling one every time, maybe even for more money. When you bring joy and light to people's days, you get liked, and liked people get the sale.
- Takeaway for interviews too: be the candidate people *enjoy* being around.

## Slide 27 — Social proof 👥
- People look to others to decide what's safe. Testimonials, user counts, reviews, recognizable logos.
- Nobody wants to be first; everybody wants to be the 401st.
- Practical: never launch with zero proof — get 3 testimonials before you need 300.

## Slide 28 — Authority 🎓
- Credentials and demonstrated expertise make people defer to you.
- Earn it and show it — a track record, a confident teardown, the right framing.
- Foreshadow: "Authority can also be *abused* — hold that thought; we'll see the doctor in the white coat in the Incentives session."

## Slide 29 — Scarcity ⏳
- Limited slots and real deadlines create urgency and action.
- The key word is **real**. Fake scarcity ("only 2 left!" when it's not) gets sniffed out and torches your trust permanently.

## Slide 30 — Consistency ✅
- People act consistently with what they've already done. A small commitment makes the next, bigger yes far more likely.
- Callback: that email opt-in from Part 2 isn't just a lead — it's a *first small yes* that makes the eventual purchase easier.

## Slide 31 — Unity 🫂
- Cialdini's newest principle. The deepest pull isn't "people I like" — it's "people who *are* me."
- Shared identity: family, team, hometown, alma mater, veteran, first-gen, whatever is *real*.
- Practical: find the genuine shared identity with the person. Don't fake it — people feel manufactured tribe.

## Slide 32 — It all stems from one habit
- Here's the secret the slick tactics miss: Covey's Habit 5 — *seek first to understand, then to be understood.*
- The best closers ASK far more than they pitch. Diagnose before you prescribe, like a good doctor.
- All seven Cialdini principles land 10x harder once the person genuinely feels heard. Listening *is* the technique.

## Slide 33 — Think win/win
- Covey's Habit 4. (Yes, *48 Laws of Power* teaches leverage — worth understanding — but in business, repeat games beat one-shot games.)
- A win/win sale compounds into referrals; a win/lose sale is a dead end and bad word-of-mouth.
- Play long games with people who play long games.

## Slide 34 — Tactics without trust is manipulation
- The line between a great salesperson and a sleazy one. It's not the tactics — both use the same six or seven principles.
- The difference: are you helping the customer reach *their* goal, or tricking them toward *yours*?
- Reinforce with the classics: Ziglar — help enough others get what they want; Bettger — enthusiasm and asking questions; Tracy — 80% of selling is psychology and self-image; Holmes — discipline on the fundamentals.

## Slide 35 — Part 5: My reps, unfiltered
- Transition: "Let me make this real with two stories — one where I learned detachment, one about the hardest product on earth."

## Slide 36 — The Verizon doorstep
- Tell a specific rough afternoon of rejection, and the exact moment I stopped taking the no's personally.
- The instant I detached, my body language relaxed, and my numbers actually went up.
- Lesson: the no's are the job, not a verdict on you.

## Slide 37 — Navy recruiting
- I sell the single hardest "product" there is: asking a teenager *and* their parents to commit years of their life to the military.
- No refund, enormous stakes, deeply emotional, and you're often talking past fear.
- "If you can sell that ethically — with the family's real interest at heart — you can sell anything."

## Slide 38 — Your turn: sell me something
- Shift energy up; this is interactive. "Who's brave?"

## Slide 39 — 30-second pitch
- Pick 2–3 volunteers. Each pitches a real or made-up product in 30 seconds.
- I raise one real objection. Then we repair it live, out loud, using today's tools: Which lane would you use? Which principle are you leaning on? Did you seek to understand me first?
- Keep it fun and supportive — the goal is reps, not perfection.

## Slide 40 — Homework / close
- Two assignments this week: (1) stand up a one-page "notify me" opt-in for *any* idea; (2) send 10 warm-outreach messages to people who already know you. Report back next session.
- Closing line: "You don't need permission and you don't need a finished product. You need a doorbell and the nerve to ring it. Now go ring."
