# You Don't Rise to the Occasion
### Leadership Under Pressure, From a Submarine

**Format:** ~60 min (45 min talk + 15 min Q&A)
**One-liner:** Under real pressure, you don't rise to the occasion — you fall to the level of your preparation. So raise the level. That's the whole job.
**OPSEC note:** Everything here is discussed at an unclassified, general-engineering level — buoyancy, basic ship systems, remotely-operated-valve failure modes, and human performance. No tactics, no specifics that aren't already public-domain seamanship.

---

## Hook (4 min)
- The myth: in a crisis, heroes summon something extra and rise to the moment.
- The reality I've lived: **"You do not rise to the occasion; you fall to your level of training."** When the alarm goes off, you don't get smarter — you execute whatever you've already burned into muscle memory. Calm is a *product of preparation*, not a personality trait.
- On a submarine, "not knowing the procedure" or freezing isn't embarrassing — it can get everyone killed. That stakes-level clarifies what leadership actually is.

## Part 1 — Preparation Is the Performance (10 min)
- I ran **countless drills in simulators** — steam-line ruptures, casualties of every kind — over and over, until the response was automatic. The drills are boring on purpose. Boring is the point.
- **The equation of clutch performance:** *Performance under pressure = (Preparation × Knowledge) executed calmly.* Raise either factor and your floor rises with it.
- "Coming in clutch" looks like talent from the outside. From the inside it's just **a high floor** — you prepared so thoroughly that even your worst, most-stressed self is good enough.
- Question for the room: *think of a time you cracked under pressure, and a time you nailed it. What was actually different?* (Almost always: preparation, not courage.)

## Part 2 — Sea Stories: Performing Under Pressure (12 min)
Each of these is a real engineering casualty, told at the high level — buoyancy, fluid systems, valve failures, human response. Each illustrates that the outcome was decided *before* the alarm, by training.
- **Hydraulic rupture** — a high-pressure system lets go. Why the trained response (isolate, control, communicate) beats improvisation every time.
- **Refrigerant leak** — a confined-space hazard; calm procedure vs. panic.
- **Reverse-osmosis (water plant) repairs** — the unglamorous grind of keeping critical equipment alive; reliability as a leadership outcome.
- **Loss of depth control** — the one *real* casualty I personally had to manage (not a drill). At the unclassified level: this is fundamentally about **buoyancy** and the failure behavior of **remotely-operated valves** — when a system that should hold doesn't, the boat's control of its own depth is at stake. What made it go right was not heroics; it was people falling back on trained procedure and clear communication. *(I'll keep this at the engineering/physics level only.)*
- **The common thread:** every time it went right, it was because people performed the preparation. The drama was absorbed by the training.

## Part 3 — Leading vs. Managing (8 min)
A core distinction the students need named:
- **Management** is about *systems, procedures, and the known* — keeping the trains running, the checklist complete, the standard enforced. Essential. The submarine runs on superb management.
- **Leadership** is about *people and the unknown* — setting direction, absorbing fear so your team can function, making the call with incomplete information, taking ownership when it goes wrong.
- You need both, and you switch between them constantly. In a casualty: management keeps the procedure exact; leadership keeps the *humans* executing it from freezing.
- Tie to *7 Habits (Covey):* management is "doing things right"; leadership is "doing the right things." The ladder vs. which wall it's leaning against.

## Part 4 — Delegation vs. Abdication: Leading in a New Business (8 min)
The same lesson transplanted from the boat to a startup, via *The E-Myth Revisited (Michael E. Gerber)*:
- Gerber's insight: most small businesses fail because the founder works *in* the business (the technician doing the task) instead of *on* it (building the system that does the task).
- **Delegation** = handing off a task *with a clear system, standard, and accountability*, then verifying. The leader still owns the outcome.
- **Abdication** = throwing a task over the wall and hoping. No system, no standard, no follow-up. This is how new founders get burned and conclude "I have to do everything myself."
- The submarine taught me delegation at gunpoint: I had to trust trained watchstanders to execute, but I built and verified the *system* (the drills, the procedures, the qualifications) that made the trust safe. **You delegate the task; you never abdicate the ownership.** That's exactly what a founder must learn.

## Part 5 — How to Build Your Own High Floor (4 min)
- Pick the situations you know will be high-stakes (an interview, a pitch, a hard conversation) and *drill them* before they happen. Rehearse out loud. Anticipate the casualty.
- Raise your knowledge and preparation in calm times so your stressed self inherits a high floor.

## Close + Q&A (15 min)
- **Exercise:** name a high-pressure event coming up in your life; design the "drill" that would raise your floor for it.
- Open floor: leadership questions, military Q&A (kept at the appropriate level).
