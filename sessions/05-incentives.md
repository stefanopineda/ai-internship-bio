# Show Me the Incentive, I'll Show You the Outcome
### Incentives 101 — The Hidden Force Behind Every Behavior

**Format:** ~60 min (45 min talk + 15 min Q&A / case debate)
**One-liner:** You can measure how good something is by the outcomes it produces. Where the outcomes are bad, look closer — the incentives are misaligned. Always.

---

## Hook (4 min)
- Charlie Munger: *"Show me the incentive and I'll show you the outcome."* Once you can see incentive structures, you can't unsee them — in companies, in government, in your doctor's office, in your own habits.
- The master key for the whole session: **outcomes are the honest report card.** When results are consistently bad and smart, well-meaning people keep producing them, stop blaming the people — examine what they're being rewarded for.
- *Skin in the Game (Taleb)* runs underneath everything today: people behave differently when they bear the consequences of their own decisions.

## Part 1 — Goodhart's Law and the AI Token Leaderboard (10 min)
> **"When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure."**
- A real, blunt example I'll narrate: an attempt to drive AI adoption by **gamifying token spend** — leaderboards ranking who used the AI most.
- The intended outcome: more useful AI adoption. The *actual* outcome: people optimized for the *metric*, not the *goal*. Token spend ballooned. Costs exploded. Activity went up; value didn't.
- The lesson isn't "metrics are bad" — it's that **people optimize the exact thing you measure, not the thing you meant.** Pick the metric carelessly and you'll get exactly what you asked for and nothing you wanted.
- Application to your own life: whatever you measure yourself on (GPA, follower count, hours studied) is shaping your behavior right now — is it the thing you actually want?

## Part 2 — The Navy FITREP: How Bureaucracies Distill a Human (15 min)
A live, insider breakdown of how the Navy incentivizes its officers — and what that produces.

**What an O-3 (Lieutenant) FITREP actually is:**
- A year of your work compressed into **a handful of bullet comments** plus **numeric grades (1.0–5.0)** across performance traits: *Professional Expertise, Military Bearing/Character, Teamwork, Mission Accomplishment & Initiative, Leadership* (and Command/Organizational Climate where applicable).
- A single **promotion recommendation** ranking: *Significant Problems → Progressing → Promotable → Must Promote → Early Promote (EP).*
- Crucially, those top marks are **forced-distribution** — only so many EPs/MPs exist per peer group. You aren't just graded; you're **ranked against your shipmates.**

**What that structure incentivizes — and what it retains:**
- Competition with peers (sometimes over collaboration), because the curve is fixed.
- Optimizing for what's *visible and writable* in three bullets — high-profile, measurable wins.
- A nudge toward **risk-aversion and conformity** — hard to look "Early Promote" while taking unconventional swings that might fail.
- **Retention effect:** the system retains people who excel at the game it rewards, and can quietly push out talented non-conformists whose value doesn't fit in three bullets and a number.
- **No system is neutral.** The FITREP isn't evil — but it produces exactly the officers it's designed to produce. Ask the same of any organization you join: *what does the evaluation form actually reward?*

## Part 3 — Politics, Promotion, and Why Good People Do Frustrating Things (5 min)
- Office politics isn't a character flaw of "political" people — it's a rational response to how advancement is structured. Change the promotion criteria and the "politics" change with it.
- When you join a company, read the incentive structure before you judge the behavior.

## Part 4 — US Healthcare: Authority Meets the Income Statement (12 min)
The most personal example — I'll tie it to my daughter's recent birth, where I challenged doctors and watched incentives drive recommendations in real time.

**The structural reality (not a conspiracy — just incentives):**
- Most hospitals are **for-profit institutions.** Pull up a real one live: **HCA Healthcare**, a ~$60B+ publicly traded hospital company, whose 10-K mission statement reads *"Above all else, we are committed to the care and improvement of human life."* Beautiful — *and* it is legally accountable to shareholders to make money. Hold both truths at once.
- They're **required to serve Medicare/Medicaid and stabilize ER patients (EMTALA)** — often **loss leaders** reimbursed below cost. To stay profitable, the margin has to come from somewhere else.
- **The labrum example:** a partial labrum tear could be treated with supplements, stretching, and a structured rehab routine — *or* a ~$50K surgery. Both can be defended medically.
- Now add **authority bias** (Cialdini): the person recommending is a credentialed expert in a white coat. There is a **non-zero chance** the institution's need for revenue subtly tilts the recommendation toward the surgery — and a sincere doctor may not even feel the tilt.
- **The point is not "doctors are bad."** They went to school to help, and they'll tell you what they genuinely think is best. But "best" can be quietly colored by the incentive structure around them. Many patients see through it; many don't. **Knowing the incentives lets you ask better questions** ("what would you do if it were your shoulder, and what's the conservative path first?").

## Part 5 — Turning the Lens on Yourself (4 min)
- Design your *own* incentives on purpose: what you reward yourself for, who you're around, what you measure. You will become what you incentivize.

## Close + Q&A (15 min)
- **Case debate:** I give a scenario (a startup's sales-commission plan, a school's grading policy); the room predicts the behavior it'll produce.
- Open floor.
