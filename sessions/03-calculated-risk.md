# Heads I Win, Tails I Don't Lose Much
### Building Optionality *Before* You Need It

**Format:** ~60 min (45 min talk + 15 min Q&A)
**One-liner:** Risk isn't about being brave. It's about rigging the game so the downside is small and the upside is enormous — and then playing it over and over.

---

## Hook (4 min)
- I'm leaving a stable Navy career in 16 months to chase entrepreneurship — with a wife and a newborn. That sounds reckless. It isn't. I spent years quietly building the safety net that makes the bet *low-risk*.
- The skill isn't "taking risks." It's **structuring** them. Today I'll show you the framework I use, the investor who taught it to me, and how to apply it to the most valuable thing you own: your time.

## Part 1 — The Dhandho Principle (12 min)
From *The Dhandho Investor (Mohnish Pabrai)*. *Dhandho* (Gujarati) = "endeavors that create wealth." The core idea:
> **"Heads, I win; tails, I don't lose much."**
- **Low risk, high uncertainty** is the sweet spot — and it's the opposite of how most people think. Most people flee uncertainty. But uncertainty (a wide range of outcomes) is not the same as risk (permanent loss of capital). When something is *already* priced for disaster, you can have huge uncertainty with almost no downside.
- Pabrai's image: a business trading near its *scrap value* can't fall much further — the floor is in. But if conditions improve, the upside is multiples. Asymmetry.

**Pabrai's own story (the living example):**
- Founded his IT company **TransTech in 1991** with ~$30K from his own 401(k) and ~$70K of **credit-card debt** — a calculated bet where the downside was bounded (he could rebuild) and the upside was open.
- His old employer told him they'd take him back if he failed — *he had a floor.* That's optionality: he only had upside to chase because his downside was capped.
- He bootstrapped it to ~$20M in revenue and **sold it in 2000 for ~$20M** (~**$37M** in today's dollars — I'll cite the inflation math live so we're honest about the number).
- He then took that capital and ran the **Dhandho playbook in public markets** — cloning Buffett, betting only where the downside was already priced in.
- **I have a photo with Pabrai from the Berkshire Hathaway annual shareholder meeting — I'll put it on screen.** Meeting the people who actually do this stuff is itself a form of optionality.

## Part 2 — From Money to Time: Investing the Asset You Can't Get Back (12 min)
The Dhandho lens isn't just for money. **Your time and attention are capital, and most people invest them terribly.**
- Every action you take is shaping your brain and your skill stack — you are *always* building something, even when you don't mean to.
- Ask of any activity: **"Is this building durable capacity, or just consuming hours?"**
  - Example I live: *Is building a new automation that saves me time, or recording a YouTube video that compounds, a better use of this hour than a one-off task?* Usually the asset-building option wins, even when it's slower today.
- **Probabilistic thinking** (from Taleb's *Incerto* — *Antifragile, Fooled by Randomness, Skin in the Game*): judge decisions by the full distribution of outcomes, not the one that happened. Seek **asymmetry** — bets where being wrong costs little and being right pays a lot. Be *antifragile*: structure your life so volatility helps you instead of ruining you.

## Part 3 — The Casino Question (8 min)
The reframe that changes how you choose what to do:
> **In every activity, skill, and venture — are you the gambler, or are you the house?**
- The gambler plays games where the odds are structurally against them. The house plays games where time and math are on its side.
- **How to find "house" games:** the deeper your understanding of the world — what's changing, where things are going — the better you can position. The goal is to **predict bottlenecks and build capacity for them before everyone else is scrambling.**
  - AI right now is a house game for people who learn to build with it early. The bottleneck is "people who can actually ship with these tools." Build that capacity now and you're the house.
- My rental properties (Virginia and South Carolina) as a worked example: where's the asymmetry, where's the downside floor, where am I the house vs. just a landlord taking on a second job?

## Part 4 — Building the Net Before the Leap (5 min)
- Optionality is built in calm weather, not in the storm. I built my runway over years so that *today's* "risky" move is actually safe.
- The sequence: secure the floor (savings, skills, a fallback) → *then* take the swing. Cap your downside first; the upside takes care of itself.
- This is why the personal-finance waterfall and this talk are the same lesson from two angles.

## Close + Q&A (15 min)
- **Exercise:** each student names one thing they spend time on and labels it "gambler" or "house," then names one bottleneck they could build capacity for now.
- Open floor: how to evaluate a specific bet, rentals, "should I take this internship/job/risk."
